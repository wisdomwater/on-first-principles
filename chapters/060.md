## 60 — When Kindness Reveals the Heart

> *“The goodness of God leadeth thee to repentance.”*

There is a surprising truth about kindness: it does not affect everyone in the same way.

For one person, patience awakens gratitude. A second chance becomes the beginning of transformation. Mercy softens the heart and creates a desire to become better. Yet another person responds very differently. Instead of gratitude, kindness produces complacency. Patience is mistaken for weakness. Mercy is treated as permission. What was meant to encourage growth becomes an excuse to remain unchanged.

This helps explain what it means to say that God hardened Pharaoh's heart. The hardening was not the result of God pouring stubbornness into him. Rather, God continued to show patience. Warning followed warning. Opportunity followed opportunity. Signs and wonders accumulated before him. Each encounter gave Pharaoh another chance to reconsider. Yet instead of yielding, he became increasingly entrenched in his resistance.

We see similar patterns in ordinary life. A wise parent may endure a child's misbehavior for a time, hoping patience will lead to maturity. Sometimes it does. Other times the child mistakes patience for indifference and grows more reckless. In such a case the parent's kindness did not create the problem, but it revealed what was already developing within the child. The same act produced different results because it encountered a different response.

This is the point the Apostle Paul makes when he says that the goodness of God leads people toward repentance. Goodness has a purpose. It is meant to awaken the heart. Yet people remain free in how they respond to that goodness. One person is humbled by it. Another despises it. One sees mercy as an invitation to change. Another sees it as an opportunity to continue as before.

Notice how this understanding preserves both divine goodness and human responsibility. God remains patient, generous, and just. The fault does not lie in the mercy that was offered. The decisive factor is the response to that mercy. Pharaoh's heart was revealed through repeated encounters with divine patience. The same signs that could have led him toward humility instead exposed the depth of his resistance.

This insight extends beyond Pharaoh's story. Most of us can look back and recognize moments when life offered us opportunities to change. A warning we ignored. A kindness we took for granted. A truth we resisted hearing. Yet we can also remember moments when grace broke through our defenses and softened something that had become rigid within us. Divine goodness is constantly at work, not forcing the heart in one direction or another, but inviting it. Whether that invitation becomes a source of healing or a cause of further hardening depends, in no small measure, upon how we choose to receive it.
