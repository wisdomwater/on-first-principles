## 71 — The Soul Is Not Finished

> *"If a man purge himself, he shall be a vessel unto honor."*

We often speak about people as though they are fixed. One person is generous. Another is selfish. One is faithful. Another is corrupt. Given enough time, these descriptions can begin to feel permanent, as though a person's present condition reveals what they truly are.

But consider how much of life resists that conclusion. The disciplined person can become careless. The bitter person can become compassionate. Someone who once inspired others can lose their way, while someone who seemed hopeless can rise into wisdom and goodness. Human beings are not statues carved once and left unchanged. We are living creatures, always becoming something.

This helps explain why no one should place too much confidence in either their virtues or their failures. A person who has reached a noble place in life is not guaranteed to remain there. Goodness must continue to be chosen. At the same time, a person who has fallen into destructive habits should never conclude that the story is over. As long as the soul remains capable of turning toward the good, change remains possible.

Taken seriously, this means that no label can fully define a person. The outsider may become an insider. The one who seems distant from God may draw near. The one who appears secure may drift away. The boundaries we draw between people are often far less permanent than we imagine. What matters most is not where someone stands today but the direction in which they are moving.

This raises a difficult question. If growth depends upon us, why does Scripture sometimes speak as though everything depends upon God? Yet in other places it speaks as though the outcome rests entirely upon our choices. The answer is that both truths describe different sides of the same reality. We do not transform ourselves apart from God's help. The very capacity to seek truth, desire goodness, and choose wisely is itself a gift. Yet neither does God change us without our participation. The gift must be received. The invitation must be answered.

Think of a sailboat crossing open water. The wind provides the power, but the sailor must still raise the sail and set the course. Without the wind, the boat cannot move. Without the sailor, the wind accomplishes little. Growth works in much the same way. Divine grace and human freedom are not rivals competing for control. They are partners in the work of transformation.

The soul, then, should never be viewed as finished. No failure need be final, and no achievement should lead to complacency. At every moment we are being shaped by what we choose, what we love, and what we pursue. The story of a human life is not the story of what a person has been, but of what that person is still becoming.
