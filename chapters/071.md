## 71 — From One Lump

> *"From the same lump is made both a vessel of honor and a vessel of dishonor."*

Why do some people seem drawn toward goodness while others struggle against it? Why does one life appear blessed while another seems burdened from the very beginning? Questions like these have troubled thoughtful people for as long as human beings have reflected on justice, freedom, and the ways of God.

One answer is to imagine that people are fundamentally different at their core—that some are made for goodness while others are not. But consider what follows from that idea. If some souls are created noble and others defective from the start, then responsibility begins to disappear. A person becomes little more than what they were made to be. Praise and blame lose their meaning because the outcome was settled before the story even began.

A better way is to begin with the conviction that all rational beings share the same essential nature. Think of a potter working with a single lump of clay. The vessels may differ in shape, purpose, beauty, or condition, but the clay itself is the same. In the same way, no soul begins as a different kind of being. Whatever differences we observe arise not from a different nature, but from the choices, habits, and formation that shape a life over time.

This understanding preserves something precious: both justice and hope. Justice remains because our condition is connected to how we have lived and responded to the good. Hope remains because no condition is permanent. A vessel honored today may become dishonored through neglect. A vessel dishonored today may be restored through repentance, growth, and renewed faithfulness. No one is trapped forever in either success or failure.

This also sheds light on a difficult passage in which Paul rebukes those who argue with God. The problem is not honest questioning. Faith has always included questions. Moses spoke with God openly. The saints wrestle, seek, and inquire. The rebuke is directed toward a different spirit—the spirit that asks not to understand but to accuse, not to learn but to justify itself. There is a difference between a student seeking wisdom and a debater seeking victory.

Seen this way, the world becomes less a courtroom where destinies are assigned and more a workshop where souls are being shaped. Whatever your condition today, it is not the final word. The honored vessel must remain vigilant. The broken vessel must not despair. We all come from the same clay, and the work of becoming is not yet finished.
