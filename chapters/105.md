## 105 — Reading with Both Eyes Open

> *“The passages which are historically true are far more numerous than those which contain a purely spiritual meaning.”*

By this point, a misunderstanding could easily arise. If some passages are symbolic, and if some commands seem impossible when taken literally, does that mean we should stop taking scripture seriously in its plain sense? Not at all. The challenge is not choosing between the literal and the spiritual. The challenge is learning when each is appropriate.

Many teachings require no elaborate interpretation. "Do not kill." "Do not steal." "Do not bear false witness." "Honor your father and mother." These commands speak clearly enough on their own. The same is true of many teachings of Jesus and the apostles. Avoid lust. Be patient. Encourage the discouraged. Help the weak. These instructions are not riddles waiting to be solved. They are truths meant to be lived.

Yet alongside these clear teachings are passages that raise questions. Some stories contain puzzling details. Some commands seem impossible if interpreted mechanically. Some statements appear disconnected from their immediate context. When we encounter such passages, wisdom requires patience. We should neither force a literal reading where it does not fit nor rush to dismiss the text altogether. Instead, we must learn to read carefully, asking what the passage is trying to accomplish.

Think of scripture as a landscape rather than a single road. Some paths are straight and obvious. Others wind through forests and hills, inviting exploration. A traveler who treats every path as identical will soon become lost. In the same way, a reader who insists that every passage must function in exactly the same manner may miss much of what the text is offering.

This requires effort. Deep reading is rarely passive. We must pay attention to context, notice recurring themes, and allow scripture to interpret scripture. When a passage seems impossible in one place, another passage may provide the key that unlocks its meaning. Patterns emerge. Symbols repeat. Images echo one another across different books and generations. Gradually the reader begins to perceive a deeper unity running beneath the surface.

The goal is not to become clever interpreters who discover hidden meanings everywhere. The goal is to become attentive readers who recognize that divine wisdom may speak in more than one way. Sometimes truth comes as a direct command. Sometimes it comes through a story. Sometimes it comes through an image that lingers in the mind long after the words have been read.

To read scripture well, then, is to keep both eyes open. One eye sees the plain teaching, the historical events, and the practical instruction. The other looks for the deeper realities toward which those things point. When both are working together, the text becomes richer and more alive. The letter provides a foundation, while the spirit reveals a greater horizon beyond it.
