## 94 — The Threefold Reading

> *"One must portray the meaning of the sacred writings in a threefold way upon one's own soul."*

If Scripture contains depths beyond its literal wording, then how should we approach it? The answer begins with a simple observation: human beings themselves are layered creatures. We have bodies that live in the physical world. We have minds that reason, learn, and make choices. And we possess a deeper spiritual capacity that longs for truth, meaning, and communion with God. It should not surprise us, then, if sacred writings speak to all these dimensions at once.

A story can be true on more than one level. At the most obvious level, it may describe events that occurred in history. We read about journeys, kingdoms, families, failures, and victories. These narratives provide instruction through concrete examples. They teach us through actions and consequences, much as a parent teaches a child through stories.

Yet many passages also contain a second layer. Beyond the events themselves lie lessons about the inner life. The people, places, and situations described in Scripture often mirror experiences within our own hearts. Battles become pictures of moral struggle. Journeys become images of growth and transformation. Exile and return become symbols of wandering away from wisdom and finding our way home again. The text begins to speak not only about others but about us.

Then there is a still deeper layer. At this level, the focus shifts from human behavior to spiritual reality itself. The stories become windows through which we glimpse the nature of God, the purpose of creation, and the destiny toward which all things are moving. The visible narrative becomes a doorway into invisible truths. What first appeared to be a story about a particular people or event opens into a vision of something universal and eternal.

Consider a great cathedral. A visitor may first admire the stone walls and stained-glass windows. Another may begin to appreciate the architecture and symbolism woven into the design. Yet a third may experience the building as a place that awakens awe and directs the soul toward God. The structure remains the same, but different levels of meaning become visible as understanding deepens.

This is why spiritual reading requires more than gathering information. The purpose is not merely to learn what a text says. It is to allow the text to shape the whole person. The stories inform the mind, challenge the conscience, and awaken the spirit. A reader who engages only one layer receives only part of the gift.

The richest reading happens when all three dimensions work together. The historical story grounds us. The moral meaning transforms us. The spiritual vision lifts us beyond ourselves. Scripture then becomes more than a collection of ancient writings. It becomes a living teacher, speaking to every level of human existence and drawing us steadily toward wisdom.
