## 84 — When God Becomes All

> *"God shall be all in all."*

Few phrases in Scripture are as sweeping or mysterious as the promise that God will one day be "all in all." It points toward the final goal of creation, but what does it actually mean? Surely it does not mean that every object simply becomes God, or that individuality disappears into some vast spiritual ocean. The question is deeper than that. What would it look like for a rational being to be so completely united with God that nothing remains opposed to his life, wisdom, or goodness?

Imagine a mind entirely free from resentment, fear, selfishness, confusion, and every other distortion that now clouds human perception. Imagine a heart so filled with truth and love that it no longer turns toward anything destructive. In such a state, every thought would be illuminated by God, every desire shaped by God, every act flowing from God. This is the vision being described. God becomes "all" not because the soul ceases to exist, but because the soul has become wholly transparent to divine life.

Notice how this connects to the beginning of the story. Humanity first encountered a world where good and evil stood side by side, where choices carried consequences, and where wisdom had to be learned through experience. But if the end resembles the beginning restored and perfected, then the struggle with evil eventually comes to an end. The soul no longer needs to know good by contrast with evil because evil itself has been overcome. What remains is a simple, direct participation in the good.

This raises another question. What kind of existence could sustain such a life? Paul speaks of a "spiritual body," and this becomes the focus of the discussion. The idea is not that existence becomes less real, but that it becomes more fully alive. Just as a seed and a flowering tree are connected yet vastly different in glory, so the present body and the future body belong to the same story while differing enormously in their condition. What is now burdened by weakness, decay, and limitation will be transformed into something capable of expressing the fullness of life God intends.

The transformation extends beyond humanity. The entire creation is moving toward unity. Diversity itself is not a problem, but the divisions born from corruption, conflict, and death are temporary. The goal is a harmony in which all things find their proper place within God's life. As this unity deepens, the forces that oppose it gradually disappear. This is why even death is called an enemy destined for destruction. The end of death means the end of everything that fragments, wounds, and separates.

Perhaps the most striking claim is that destruction does not necessarily mean annihilation. When death is destroyed, what vanishes is its power to harm. When evil is overcome, what disappears is its hostility toward the good. The Creator does not abandon what he has made. Instead, he heals, transforms, and restores. Just as the body that enters the grave is not lost forever but raised in a new and more glorious form, so the whole movement of creation points toward renewal rather than extinction.

The promise that God will be "all in all" is therefore not a vision of things ceasing to exist. It is a vision of things becoming what they were always meant to be. Every shadow is gone. Every division is healed. Every creature lives fully within the light of the One from whom all life came in the first place.
