## 97 — Learning to See Beneath the Words

> *"These things happened unto them figuratively, and they were written for our sake."*

How do we know that Scripture contains deeper meanings? The answer is surprisingly simple: Scripture itself teaches us to read this way.

Consider a law that instructs farmers not to muzzle an ox while it treads grain. At first glance, the command seems straightforward. It protects a working animal from unnecessary hardship. Yet when Paul reflects on this passage, he asks a startling question: Is the command only about oxen? He suggests that a deeper principle is being revealed—that those who labor deserve to share in the fruit of their work. The law remains true on its surface level, but it also points beyond itself.

This illustrates an important principle. A passage may begin with something concrete and practical, yet still contain wisdom that reaches much further. The visible lesson becomes a doorway into a larger truth. The ox is real, but the command is not merely about oxen. The story is real, but the story is not merely about the story.

The same pattern appears throughout the biblical writings. Events, objects, rituals, and institutions often function like shadows cast by realities that are greater than themselves. A shadow is not unreal. It is simply incomplete. It hints at a form that extends beyond what can be seen. In the same way, many features of Scripture point toward truths that are spiritual, universal, and enduring.

Think about the difference between a blueprint and a building. A blueprint is valuable, but its purpose is not to be admired for its own sake. It exists because it points to something larger. If someone spent their life studying the lines and measurements without ever understanding the structure being built, they would miss the blueprint's purpose. Scripture often works this way. The visible forms direct our attention toward invisible realities.

This helps explain why the apostles so frequently revisited familiar stories and found new depths within them. A rock in the wilderness became a symbol of Christ. Historical events became pictures of spiritual realities. Ancient covenants became illustrations of truths that extended far beyond the original circumstances. They were not abandoning the original stories. They were uncovering what the stories had been pointing toward all along.

The challenge for us is similar. We can read a passage and ask only, "What does this say?" Or we can continue asking, "What does this reveal?" The first question is necessary. The second often leads to wisdom. Scripture becomes richest when we learn to hold both together—respecting the words while also looking through them toward the reality they illuminate.

In this way, the text becomes more than a record of past events. It becomes a living guide. Every story, command, and image invites us to look deeper, not because the surface meaning is unimportant, but because the surface meaning is often the beginning of a much larger discovery.
