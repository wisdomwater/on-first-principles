## 47 — The Fire We Carry Within

> *"Walk in the light of your fire and in the flame which you have kindled for yourselves."*

When many people hear about judgment, they imagine a punishment imposed entirely from the outside. They picture a sentence handed down by a distant authority and carried out upon the soul. But what if the deepest judgment comes not from something foreign to us, but from the truth of our own lives being fully revealed?

Before exploring that question, consider how transformation works in nature. A seed buried in the ground does not disappear. Something hidden within it survives the decay of its outer form and eventually gives rise to a new plant. In the same way, the body that dies is not the final form of who we are. A deeper life remains within it. Through the power of God, that life becomes the source from which a new and transformed existence emerges. What rises is still connected to the person who lived before, yet it has been reshaped for a higher mode of life.

This transformed existence, however, does not erase the consequences of how we have lived. Every action leaves a mark upon the soul. Every choice shapes habits, desires, and patterns of thought. Much of this remains hidden from us now. We justify ourselves, forget, distract ourselves, or simply fail to see clearly. Yet imagine what would happen if every illusion fell away and a person suddenly saw the truth of his own life without distortion.

That, we are told, is the beginning of judgment.

The imagery of fire points toward this experience. Fire burns away what cannot endure. It exposes what is genuine and what is false. The "eternal fire" is therefore understood not merely as an external flame but as the painful encounter between the soul and the truth. The sins we cultivate become the fuel. The selfishness we defended, the wounds we refused to heal, the desires we allowed to govern us—all rise before us with a clarity that can no longer be avoided. Conscience itself becomes a witness.

Even in this life we catch glimpses of such fire. Jealousy can consume a person from within. Unresolved anger can feel like a burning fever. Shame can torment the mind long after an act has passed. We already know that certain states of the soul create their own suffering. The difference is that in ordinary life we can often escape, deny, or numb that pain. In the presence of perfect truth, no such escape remains.

Yet the purpose of this fire is not destruction for its own sake. Throughout the passage, the image quietly shifts from that of a prison to that of a physician's office. A surgeon may cut in order to heal. A doctor may prescribe bitter medicine in order to restore health. In the same way, divine judgment is portrayed as a remedy for the soul's disorder. The fire reveals, purifies, and restores. It burns because healing a deeply damaged soul is not always painless.

What emerges is a striking vision of God's justice. Judgment is real. Sin matters. Nothing is ignored or excused. Yet the final goal is not vengeance but restoration. The fire of God is not opposed to his mercy. It is one of the forms that mercy takes when it encounters all that must be healed.
