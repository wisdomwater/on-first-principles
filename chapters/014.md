## 14 — The Slow Drift

> *“If they become negligent, they gradually sink to a lower level.”*

Most losses happen slowly.

A skilled musician does not wake up one morning having forgotten everything they once knew. A physician does not suddenly lose years of training overnight. Knowledge fades through neglect. Practice becomes less frequent. Attention wanders. Interest cools. Small omissions accumulate until abilities that once seemed second nature become difficult to recover. The loss is real, but it arrives so gradually that it often goes unnoticed until much later.

This ordinary experience becomes the starting point for a much larger reflection. What is true of knowledge may also be true of the soul. Spiritual decline rarely begins with a dramatic rejection of what is good. More often it begins with distraction, indifference, or carelessness. A person stops attending to what nourishes life. The practices that once sustained wisdom and clarity are neglected. Little by little, the soul drifts away from the source of its vitality.

This principle applies to the entire realm of rational beings. All rational creatures are originally created for the contemplation and enjoyment of God. Their life consists in participating in divine wisdom and goodness. Yet because they possess freedom, they can also become inattentive. They can cease to direct themselves toward the highest good. The result is not an immediate catastrophe but a gradual descent.

Let's imagine humans moving down the side of a mountain. As they descend through various levels they move further from their original condition. The views the once held begin to be obscured by the surrounding environment. The light found on the mountaintop is hidden in shadow. Separation from God is not primarily a punishment imposed from outside. It is the natural consequence of turning away from the source of life, just as darkness follows when one moves away from a light.

What is striking is that decline is never irreversible. If someone notices their drift early enough and returns to the discipline they have neglected, much can be recovered. Knowledge that has begun to fade can be renewed. Habits that have weakened can be strengthened. The same is true spiritually. The soul may wander, but it can also return. What has been neglected can once again become the object of attention.

This is a theme of creation itself. If God is eternally good, then God's goodness must always be active. A musician naturally makes music. A teacher naturally teaches. Likewise, divine goodness naturally gives life, sustains, and blesses. God does not become a creator at a particular moment because he suddenly decides to be generous. Rather, generosity belongs to his very nature.

For this reason, all creation exists eternally within divine wisdom before it appears in visible form. Just as an architect holds a building in mind before construction begins, God eternally knows and delights in the fullness of creation within his wisdom. The world unfolds in time, but its meaning is rooted in something timeless.

There is both warning and comfort in this. We become what we repeatedly attend to. Neglect slowly diminishes us, while faithful attention gradually restores us. The soul's journey is shaped not merely by dramatic choices but by countless small acts of orientation. Day by day we either drift from the light or turn toward it. And because the source of that light never ceases to shine, return is always possible.
