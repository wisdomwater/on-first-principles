## 104 — Not Everything Is Symbolic

> *“The passages which are historically true are far more numerous than those which contain a purely spiritual meaning.”*

Whenever people discover that a text contains symbolism, there is a temptation to swing to the opposite extreme. If some stories point beyond themselves, perhaps everything is symbolic. If some commands carry a deeper meaning, perhaps none of them should be taken seriously in their plain sense. But that would be a mistake.

The fact that scripture contains deeper layers of meaning does not mean its historical and practical dimensions disappear. A person who discovers that a map contains hidden notes does not conclude that the roads themselves are imaginary. The deeper meaning adds to the surface meaning; it does not automatically erase it.

This is an important balance to maintain. Some passages seem to resist a purely literal reading. Commands appear that cannot reasonably be carried out. Images appear that seem designed to provoke reflection rather than describe physical reality. Yet alongside these passages stand countless others that present straightforward history, practical wisdom, and moral instruction. Abraham was buried in a particular place. Jerusalem was a real city. Families lived, traveled, struggled, and died. These things belong to the actual story of human history.

The same is true of moral teaching. Consider the command to honor one's father and mother. Nothing about this requires us to search for hidden symbolism before obeying it. The command is wise on its face. It speaks directly to human relationships and the health of communities. Its value is immediately apparent. To insist that every passage must conceal a secret meaning would be just as misguided as insisting that none of them do.

Reading scripture well therefore requires discernment. We must learn to recognize what kind of passage stands before us. Some passages function like windows, inviting us to see realities beyond the visible words. Others function more like foundations, giving us solid truths to build upon. Wisdom lies not in forcing every text into a single category but in allowing each one to serve its intended purpose.

This is true of life as well. Some experiences carry lessons that are obvious from the start. Others reveal their meaning only after years of reflection. A friendship, a loss, a failure, or a success may contain both an outward event and an inward significance. Mature understanding grows from learning to appreciate both. We do not reject the event because it has meaning, nor do we ignore the meaning because the event was real.

The goal, then, is not to choose between the literal and the spiritual. The goal is to become attentive enough to recognize when each is being asked of us. Some passages teach us through plain instruction. Others teach us through symbol, mystery, and reflection. Scripture contains both because human beings need both. We need truths we can stand upon and truths that invite us to keep exploring.
