## 91 — Reading Beyond the Surface

> *"Many errors have arisen because the method of interpreting the divine writings is unknown to the multitude."*

When we first encounter a sacred text, it is natural to assume that its meaning lies entirely on the surface. We read the words and expect them to describe events exactly as they appear. Yet many misunderstandings begin precisely here. If a passage speaks of mountains, wolves, cities, or fire, we assume it must be talking about mountains, wolves, cities, or fire. But what if the words are pointing beyond themselves? What if the visible image is meant to lead us toward a deeper reality?

Consider the expectations many people held regarding the coming of Christ. They searched the prophets for signs and concluded that every detail must occur in a literal and visible way. If the Messiah was promised to bring freedom, they expected political liberation. If a peaceful kingdom was described, they expected animals to change their behavior and predators to lie peacefully beside their prey. When these things did not happen in the manner they anticipated, they concluded that Jesus could not be the promised one.

The problem was not that they took the Scriptures seriously. The problem was that they took only one layer of meaning seriously. They focused so intently on the outward image that they missed the reality to which the image pointed. A prophecy about peace may describe wolves and lambs, but perhaps its deepest concern is not zoology. Perhaps it is speaking about what happens when hostility gives way to reconciliation, when violence yields to harmony, and when opposing forces learn to dwell together in peace.

The same mistake can occur in the opposite direction. Some readers encounter passages that describe God's anger, jealousy, or repentance and conclude that God must be ruled by changing emotions just as human beings are. They take every expression at face value and build an understanding of God from the literal wording alone. Yet Scripture often speaks in human language because it is addressing human minds. The words are true, but they invite reflection. They call us to ask what deeper reality is being revealed through these images and descriptions.

Think of a parent speaking to a young child. The parent may use simple pictures and familiar examples, not because the reality itself is simple, but because it is the best way to communicate something beyond the child's present understanding. In much the same way, sacred writings often meet us where we are and then invite us to grow beyond our first impressions.

The lesson is not that the words of Scripture matter less. It is that they matter more. The surface meaning is often the doorway rather than the destination. Wisdom begins when we learn to look through the image toward the reality it reveals. The greatest errors arise not from taking Scripture too seriously, but from stopping at the first layer and never continuing the journey inward.
