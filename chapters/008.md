## 8 — Learning to See the Light

> *“The only-begotten Son is the brightness of this light.”*

Imagine trying to look directly at the sun after spending your entire life in darkness. The brilliance would not illuminate you; it would overwhelm you. Your eyes would be unable to bear what they were seeing. Before you could gaze upon such light, your vision would need to be strengthened and gradually accustomed to its brightness.

This image helps explain why Christ is called the radiance or brightness of God. If God is the source of all light, then Christ is the light made accessible to us. Not a lesser light, but the same light reaching us in a form we can receive. Through him, what would otherwise remain beyond our capacity becomes approachable. The infinite does not cease to be infinite, but it comes near enough to be known.

Think about how we learn anything profound. A teacher does not begin with the most difficult lesson. A good teacher meets the student where they are and gradually leads them forward. In the same way, Christ reveals God not by overwhelming human understanding but by patiently illuminating it. He removes what clouds our vision and guides us toward truths we could not have grasped on our own.

This is why Christ is called the Way, the Truth, the Life, and the Light. These are not separate roles but different expressions of the same work. He is the Way because he leads us toward God. He is the Truth because he reveals reality as it truly is. He is the Life because he restores what death has damaged. He is the Light because he enables us to perceive what was previously hidden. Every title points toward the same purpose: helping finite creatures participate in the life of God.

But how can the Son be called the image of God if God is beyond all comprehension? His answer is striking. The image does not diminish the original; it reveals it. If a reality were so vast that no one could perceive it directly, an exact representation could make it known. The representation would not replace the reality but would allow people to encounter it according to their capacity.

This is how we can understand the incarnation. The eternal Word did not cease to be what he was. Rather, he entered the limits of human life so that human beings could begin to perceive what would otherwise remain beyond them. In the life of Jesus, the immeasurable God becomes visible in human actions, human words, human compassion, and human faithfulness. The infinite shines through the finite without being reduced to it.

The result is that knowing Christ becomes a genuine path to knowing God. Not because Christ stands between humanity and God as a barrier, but because he serves as a bridge. The radiance leads us to the light from which it comes. The image leads us to the reality it reflects. Through Christ, the divine mystery becomes not smaller, but more accessible.

The goal of spiritual growth, then, is not to master God intellectually. It is to allow our vision to be healed. Little by little, the radiance trains our eyes until we can receive more of the light that has always been shining.
