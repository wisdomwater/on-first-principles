## 32 — Can Goodness and Justice Be Divided?

> *“They say that the Father of our Lord Jesus Christ is good, but the God of the law and the prophets is just.”*

People often divide the world into two kinds of virtues. On one side stands kindness: mercy, compassion, forgiveness, and generosity. On the other stands justice: accountability, consequences, and the requirement that actions matter. We tend to imagine that a person must lean toward one or the other. Too much mercy seems soft. Too much justice seems harsh.

Some teachers in Origen's day applied this distinction even to God himself. They claimed that the God revealed by Jesus was purely good and merciful, while the God revealed in the ancient Scriptures was merely just—a ruler who rewarded the righteous and punished the wicked. In their view, these could not be the same God.

At first, this sounds plausible. After all, the Old Testament contains stories of judgment, while the Gospels contain many stories of forgiveness. Yet the argument begins to unravel when we look more carefully at what goodness and justice actually mean.

Consider what happens when goodness is separated entirely from justice. If goodness means simply giving pleasant things to everyone regardless of what they do, then consequences disappear. Actions cease to matter. Right and wrong become little more than preferences. A parent who never corrects a child may appear kind in the moment, but eventually that kindness becomes neglect. What first looked like mercy turns out to be indifference.

Now consider the opposite mistake. Suppose justice means merely giving suffering to the wicked and rewards to the good. Such a view quickly becomes cold and mechanical. Justice turns into little more than punishment. Yet genuine justice seeks restoration, order, and healing. Even discipline, at its best, aims toward a good end.

This leads to a deeper insight: goodness and justice are not enemies. In fact, they need one another. Goodness without justice becomes sentimentality. Justice without goodness becomes cruelty. A truly wise ruler does not alternate between being good on one day and just on the next. Rather, justice becomes one expression of goodness, and goodness gives purpose to justice.

The problem, then, may lie in the way we read difficult passages. When we encounter stories of judgment, we immediately assume they reveal a harsh and angry God. Yet we rarely ask whether there is a deeper meaning beneath the surface. We interpret the stories in the simplest possible way and then conclude that God must be simple as well.

But perhaps the greater challenge is to hold both truths together. A God who is truly good cannot be indifferent to evil. A God who is truly just cannot cease to care for those who have gone astray. The more deeply we understand either goodness or justice, the more we discover that they belong together.

The question is not whether God is good or just. The question is whether our understanding of goodness and justice is large enough to recognize how they meet in the same divine reality.
