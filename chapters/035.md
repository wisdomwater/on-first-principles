## 35 — The Great Astonishment

> *“He emptied himself of his majestic condition and became man and dwelt among men.”*

There are truths that stretch the mind, and then there are truths that seem to break its measuring tools altogether.

We can understand, at least in part, the idea of a Creator who stands behind the universe. We can look at the order of nature, the beauty of the world, and the mystery of consciousness and sense that there is a wisdom greater than ourselves. Even if we cannot fully comprehend God, we can glimpse something of the divine through the works that surround us.

But then we come to a far stranger claim.

The same wisdom that fashioned the stars. The same divine Word through whom all things came into being. The same power that holds creation together. This, Christianity says, became a human being.

Pause for a moment and consider the magnitude of that claim.

The source of all life enters life as an infant. The wisdom that orders the universe learns to speak with a child's tongue. The one who stands beyond all weakness accepts hunger, fatigue, sorrow, misunderstanding, and suffering. The one who gives existence to all things submits to death at the hands of his own creatures.

The mind almost recoils from it.

When we think of God, we imagine majesty, power, and transcendence. When we think of humanity, we think of limitation, vulnerability, and mortality. The distance between the two seems immeasurable. Yet the heart of the Christian story is that this distance was crossed.

This creates a profound tension. Read the gospel accounts and you encounter both realities at once. Sometimes Jesus appears utterly human. He grows tired. He weeps. He suffers. He experiences fear and grief. At other moments, something shines through that seems to belong to an entirely different order of existence. He speaks with authority that astonishes crowds. He forgives sins. He reveals the character of God. He rises from the dead.

Which is he?

The answer cannot simply be one or the other. If we focus only on his humanity, we lose the wonder of who he is. If we focus only on his divinity, we risk turning his earthly life into a kind of illusion. The challenge is to hold both truths together without diminishing either.

This is why the incarnation has always inspired both devotion and humility. It is not merely a doctrine to be mastered. It is a mystery that invites contemplation. The closer we draw to it, the more we realize that our ordinary categories begin to fail us.

And perhaps that is part of the point.

There are moments when understanding advances not by reducing a mystery but by standing before it honestly. Before asking how such a thing could be possible, we must first allow ourselves to feel the wonder of it. The infinite enters the finite. The Creator enters creation. The eternal steps into time.

And the mind, finding itself unable to fully grasp such a thing, can only stand in reverent amazement.
