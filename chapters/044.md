## 44 — A World Without Favoritism

> *"He created all his creatures equal and alike."*

The deeper we look at the world, the harder certain questions become.

Why is one person born into wealth and another into poverty? Why does one child grow up surrounded by wisdom and encouragement while another is raised amid violence, ignorance, or fear? Why are some gifted with health, intelligence, and opportunity while others face obstacles from the beginning of life?

If God is both good and just, how can such differences exist?

This question troubled many thinkers in the ancient world. Some concluded that souls themselves must be fundamentally unequal. Perhaps, they reasoned, certain souls were created noble while others were created inferior. That would explain why some seem destined for greater blessings and others for hardship. Yet this solution creates a problem of its own. If God intentionally creates some beings better than others, then the inequalities of the world ultimately trace back to God's preference rather than to justice.

A different possibility begins with a simple conviction: the Creator is neither arbitrary nor partial. If God is perfectly good, then he cannot begin by favoring one soul over another. The sun does not choose to shine more brightly on one side of the sky than the other. It simply radiates light according to its nature. In the same way, divine goodness pours itself out freely. The original act of creation, therefore, must have been an act of equal generosity. Rational beings were not created as different classes of creatures. They began from the same source and received the same gift of existence.

Yet equality at the beginning does not guarantee sameness forever.

Imagine a group of travelers setting out from the same starting point. Each is given the same provisions, the same map, and the same opportunity. Over time, however, their paths diverge. Some remain focused on their destination. Others wander. Some become distracted. Others persevere. After many years, the distance between them may be enormous, even though they all began together.

This is the role freedom plays in the argument. The diversity we see in creation is not attributed to differences in the Creator but to differences in the responses of created beings. If rational creatures possess genuine freedom, then over time they can move in different directions. The resulting variety—whether among heavenly beings, earthly peoples, or the countless conditions of life—arises from those differing movements rather than from favoritism on God's part.

What makes this discussion remarkable is that it is driven by a desire to defend both divine goodness and divine justice. The world often appears unfair when viewed from a single moment in time. Some lives seem blessed while others seem burdened. Yet the argument insists that beneath the bewildering diversity of existence there is neither accident nor partiality. There is a wisdom at work that human beings cannot fully see, but which remains consistent with perfect goodness.

The challenge, then, is learning to trust that apparent inequality does not necessarily mean ultimate injustice. We see only a fragment of the story. The One who sees the whole of it may be weaving together a pattern far larger than our limited perspective can grasp.
