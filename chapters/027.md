## 27 — A School for the Soul

> *"Whether after this world there will be a course of healing and improvement."*

When we look at our lives, it is easy to feel unfinished. We make progress in one area only to discover weakness in another. We gain wisdom through suffering, yet still find ourselves repeating old mistakes. If spiritual growth is the purpose of life, one lifetime can seem remarkably short.

This observation leads to a larger question. Is the present world the whole story, or is it one chapter in a much longer process? The question is not asked out of curiosity about the future but from a desire to understand the purpose of existence itself. If God is working to restore rational creatures to harmony and wholeness, how extensive might that work be? Could it continue beyond the boundaries of the world we currently know?

The argument begins by considering endings. We often imagine the end of the world as the conclusion of all things, the moment when every question is settled forever. Yet if rational creatures remain free, and if growth remains possible, then perhaps an ending is not merely a stopping point. It may also be a transition. Just as the close of one season becomes the beginning of another, the completion of one stage of existence may prepare the way for something further.

This possibility leads to a remarkable image of creation as a kind of school for the soul. The challenges, joys, failures, and victories of life are not random events but opportunities for learning. Some advance quickly. Others require longer instruction. Some willingly cooperate with wisdom, while others resist it and learn through difficulty. Yet the goal remains the same: the gradual healing and education of rational beings until they become capable of receiving greater truth.

The discussion then turns to resurrection. What does it mean to say that the mortal becomes immortal and the corruptible becomes incorruptible? The answer offered here is not that creation is discarded and replaced. Instead, what is incomplete is transformed. Just as iron placed in a fire begins to glow with the fire's own brightness, so the soul, united with divine wisdom, gradually communicates that life to the body. Mortality gives way to life, not through destruction, but through participation in something greater.

This is why the passage speaks of the soul as clothing the body and Christ as clothing the soul. Each receives and transmits life from a higher source. The body receives life from the soul. The soul receives life from divine wisdom. Transformation occurs as that life flows ever more deeply through the whole person. The ultimate hope is not escape from creation but its renewal.

The result is a vision of reality that is both expansive and hopeful. The world is not merely a place where judgments are rendered but a place where healing occurs. History is not simply moving toward an ending but toward a fulfillment. Whether that journey extends through one age or many, the underlying purpose remains the same: to guide creation toward greater wisdom, greater freedom, and deeper participation in the life of God. In that light, the world begins to look less like a courtroom and more like a school, where every lesson—pleasant or painful—serves a larger work of restoration.
