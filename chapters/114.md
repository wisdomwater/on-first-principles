## 114 — The Soul That Never Turned Away

> *“He loved righteousness and hated iniquity.”*

If God is present everywhere, what happened when Christ became human? Did the divine Word somehow shrink itself down and fit inside a single human body? Did God abandon the rest of creation in order to be present in one place?

The answer is no. The divine Word remained what it always was—unconfined, present throughout creation, sustaining all things. Yet at the same time, that eternal Word became united with a human life in a unique and perfect way. The mystery of Christ is not that God became smaller, but that humanity became perfectly joined to God.

To understand this, think about sunlight filling the world. The light shines everywhere, yet it may enter one window more clearly than another. The source remains unchanged, but its presence can be received in different degrees. In a similar way, the divine Word is present throughout creation, yet in Jesus that presence was received without distortion, resistance, or division. What others received partially, he received completely.

This is why special attention is given to the human soul of Jesus. Like every human being, he possessed a genuine human soul. He experienced sorrow, struggle, and the realities of human life. Yet unlike every other soul, his never turned away from the good. Before knowing evil, it chose the good. Before wandering, it remained faithful. Before falling, it clung to righteousness. Through its unwavering love for what is true and good, it became perfectly united with the divine Word.

This union is what makes Christ more than merely a teacher or prophet. The divine life and the human life are joined together without confusion and without separation. The human soul becomes so filled with God's wisdom, truth, and life that it serves as the perfect expression of them. In Christ, humanity is shown not merely as it is, but as it can become when fully surrendered to God.

Yet this teaching is not offered merely to explain who Christ is. It is also meant to reveal what our own journey looks like. Christ stands before us as both revelation and example. Just as iron placed in a fire gradually takes on the fire's heat and brightness, so the human soul can increasingly participate in the life of God. We do not become divine by nature, but we can become participants in the divine life through love, faithfulness, and transformation.

This is why the Christian life is so often described as imitation. We are invited to walk as he walked, to choose what is good, to love what is true, and to allow God's life to shape our own. The path may be steep and difficult, but Christ has already traveled it. In him we see what a human life looks like when nothing resists the presence of God. And in seeing that possibility, we are given hope that our own souls, though imperfect now, may one day be filled with that same light.
