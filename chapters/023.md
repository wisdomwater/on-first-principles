## 23 — The Power to Become

> *"There is no nature which may not admit good or evil, except the nature of God."*

Why do some people grow wiser while others become bitter? Why do some recover from failure while others sink deeper into it? Questions like these assume something important: that change is possible. We may not always become better, but neither are we permanently fixed in what we are today.

This is the foundation of the argument. Every rational creature possesses the capacity to move in more than one direction. A person can learn a skill, but that does not mean everyone becomes a master. A student may become a physician, but many never do. The possibility exists, yet the outcome depends upon how that possibility is used. In the same way, every rational soul possesses the capacity for good, and every rational soul possesses the capacity for evil. What distinguishes one life from another is not the capacity itself but the choices that follow.

This principle applies even to the highest and lowest beings. The devil serves as the most dramatic example. If evil were his original nature, there would be little mystery in his fall. But the argument here is that he was once good and turned away from that good through the misuse of freedom. His fall demonstrates that goodness alone does not eliminate freedom. A creature may know the good and still reject it. Yet the opposite is equally true. A creature may have wandered far from the good and still possess the capacity to return. The door swings both ways.

Only God stands outside this pattern. Every created being can change. Every created being can gain or lose wisdom, virtue, and holiness. But God is not merely good in the way creatures are good. Goodness is not something God possesses; it is what God is. We can become wise, but wisdom can also fade. We can become righteous, yet righteousness can be lost. We can grow holy, yet holiness can diminish. These qualities come to us as gifts and habits. They are not our essence. God alone is unchanging because God alone is goodness itself.

This distinction explains much about the human experience. We often imagine that spiritual maturity means reaching a place where growth is no longer necessary. Yet wisdom behaves more like a living relationship than a possession. It deepens through attention and weakens through neglect. Just as a garden flourishes when cultivated and slowly deteriorates when abandoned, the soul grows through participation in the good. What we become tomorrow depends in part on what we love and pursue today.

The encouraging conclusion is that no creature is trapped by its current condition. The universe is not divided between those destined for greatness and those destined for failure. Angels rise through faithfulness. Corrupt powers descend through their choices. Human beings stand somewhere in the middle, continually becoming what they choose. The story of creation is therefore not a story about fixed categories but about movement. We are creatures with the power to become. And behind that long journey stands the steady kindness of God, drawing all things toward a future where sorrow fades, wisdom grows brighter, and every soul is invited further into the good.
