## 115 — Beneath the Qualities of Things

> *“A substance never exists without quality, yet it is discerned by the intellect alone.”*

When we look at the world around us, we encounter things through their qualities. We see color, feel texture, notice weight, warmth, movement, and shape. Everything we experience arrives clothed in characteristics. Yet this raises an intriguing question: beneath all those qualities, what is the thing itself?

Imagine holding a piece of wood. It may be brown, hard, dry, smooth, or rough. Now mentally remove each of those descriptions one by one. Remove the color. Remove the texture. Remove the hardness. Remove every characteristic by which you recognize it. What remains? The mind struggles to answer. We can think about the underlying reality, but we cannot easily picture it.

This is the question being explored here. Some thinkers argued that matter is nothing more than the collection of qualities we observe. Remove the qualities and nothing remains. Others believed there must be some underlying substance capable of receiving different qualities, much as clay can become a pot, a brick, or a statue. The qualities change, yet something persists beneath the changes.

To illustrate the point, consider a person. You may encounter someone while speaking, sleeping, sitting, standing, laughing, or remaining silent. None of those conditions defines who they are. They are simply states that come and go. The person remains the same individual through all of them. In a similar way, some philosophers reasoned that matter itself underlies the many changing forms we see in the world.

What particularly interests us, however, is not the technical debate but the larger conclusion that follows from it. Whatever matter may be, it is not eternal, independent, or self-existing. Everything that exists within the created order is capable of change. Water becomes vapor. Seeds become trees. Food becomes flesh. Wood becomes ash. One form gives way to another. The entire visible world is marked by transformation.

This constant change points beyond itself. If everything in creation changes, then creation cannot be its own ultimate foundation. The world is not a collection of self-sustaining realities existing on their own. It is a vast network of dependent things, all receiving their existence from a deeper source. That source is God, who alone is not merely one thing among other things, but the origin from which all things derive their being.

The more deeply we examine the world, the more mysterious it becomes. We begin with visible objects and end with questions about existence itself. We peel away layers, hoping to find the final foundation, only to discover that every created thing points beyond itself. The purpose of such inquiry is not simply to solve philosophical puzzles. It is to awaken wonder. Beneath every object, every creature, and every changing form lies the reminder that creation is not self-explanatory. It is a gift, continually sustained by a wisdom greater than anything our minds can fully comprehend.
