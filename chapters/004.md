## 4 — The God Beyond Our Grasp

> *“God is spirit, and those who worship him must worship in spirit and truth.”*

Imagine standing beside the Samaritan woman at the well. Her question seems simple enough: Where is the proper place to worship God? Is it on this mountain, as her people believed, or in Jerusalem, as the Jews taught?

Notice how unexpected the answer is. Rather than choosing one location over another, Jesus challenges the entire assumption behind the question. God is not confined to a mountain, a city, a temple, or any sacred piece of geography. If God is spirit, then true worship cannot ultimately depend upon a location. The deepest encounter with God takes place not through proximity to a place but through openness of heart and truthfulness of spirit.

This insight leads to a larger realization. Whenever we try to imagine God, we are tempted to make God into something familiar. We picture a larger version of ourselves, or we imagine a being that occupies some distant corner of the universe. Yet anything that can be fully pictured, measured, located, or described is already too small. The moment we think we have captured God within an image, we have mistaken the image for the reality.

Consider someone whose eyes have never seen more than the glow of a candle. How could you explain the brilliance of the sun? You might describe it as brighter, warmer, and more powerful, but every comparison would fall short. The person would understand something, yet the reality itself would remain beyond their experience. In much the same way, our understanding of God resembles the candle, while God's true being surpasses anything our minds can fully comprehend.

Does this mean we can know nothing about God? Not at all. Although we cannot look directly upon the source, we can perceive its rays. We know the sun exists because its light fills the world. We know something of its power because we experience its warmth. In the same way, we catch glimpses of God through the beauty of creation, the order of the universe, the movement of conscience, the experience of truth, and the transforming power of love. These are not God in his fullness, but they are signs that point toward him.

For this reason, it is wise to hold our ideas about God with both conviction and humility. We can know truly, yet never completely. Every genuine insight is valuable, but no insight exhausts the mystery. The closer we draw to God, the more we discover that the reality before us is greater than anything we previously imagined.

This is not a failure of understanding but part of the wonder itself. The finite mind was never meant to contain the infinite. Yet it can be illuminated by it. We come to know God not by placing him inside our categories, but by allowing our categories to expand until they can bear a little more of the light that shines beyond them.
