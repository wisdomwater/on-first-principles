## 66 — Opportunity and Responsibility

> *"It was not the delay of divine providence but the will of each human mind that was the cause of its ruin."*

A difficult question remains. If some people witnessed remarkable signs, heard profound teachings, and still rejected them, while others never received the same opportunities, does that seem fair? Why would some be given every advantage and others so little?

The concern is understandable. Imagine two students taking the same examination. One is given access to the lessons, the books, and the teacher, while the other is left without instruction. We would naturally question the fairness of the arrangement. In a similar way, people have long wondered why some appear to receive extraordinary spiritual opportunities while others seem to live their entire lives without them.

The answer offered here begins with a surprising observation. Sometimes people claim they would have responded differently if only they had been given the same chance as someone else. "If I had seen what they saw, I would have believed." "If I had heard what they heard, I would have changed." Yet such claims are impossible to prove. The only way to expose whether the excuse is genuine is to provide the opportunity itself.

This, according to the argument, helps explain why divine gifts are sometimes given even to those who will reject them. The purpose is not merely to save but also to reveal. When a person receives every opportunity and still refuses it, the source of the failure becomes clear. The problem cannot be blamed on a lack of evidence, a lack of teaching, or a lack of invitation. The responsibility rests within the person who received the gift and chose not to respond.

Yet even this severe conclusion is viewed through the lens of mercy. At first that may sound contradictory. How could a heavier judgment be merciful? The reasoning is that truth is better than illusion. As long as we believe our failures are caused entirely by circumstances, we remain trapped in self-deception. Growth begins when excuses lose their power. Sometimes the exposure of our resistance is itself a step toward healing because it reveals what must actually be changed.

This perspective also helps explain why God does not always reveal everything immediately. Greater light brings greater responsibility. To receive profound insight and then dismiss it is more serious than never having encountered it at all. For that reason, there may be moments when wisdom delays a revelation until a person is better prepared to receive it. The goal is not to withhold truth but to prevent truth from becoming another thing that is casually discarded.

Having addressed that question, let's turn toward another challenge. If Scripture says, "It is not of him who wills or runs, but of God who shows mercy," does human effort matter at all? Before answering, a simple question must be asked: Is it good when a person desires what is good and strives toward it? The answer seems obvious, yet it leads directly into one of the most important discussions—the relationship between human freedom and divine grace.
