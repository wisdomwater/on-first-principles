## 106 — A Map of the Soul

> *“The whole of divine scripture has a spiritual meaning, but not all a bodily meaning.”*

If scripture contains a deeper meaning beneath its stories and laws, how do we discover it? The answer begins with learning to see that the visible realities described in scripture often point beyond themselves.

Consider the history of Israel. On one level, it is the story of a real people living in a real land. There are cities, kings, battles, triumphs, failures, and generations of human lives. The names are familiar: Israel, Judah, Jerusalem, and many others. These belong to the outward history recorded in scripture.

Yet there may be another layer as well. Just as a parable uses familiar images to illuminate a deeper truth, the history of a people can become a picture of the spiritual life. The outward events remain meaningful, but they also begin to function as symbols through which larger realities are revealed. A journey through a wilderness can speak of the soul's journey toward wisdom. A divided kingdom can reflect the divided loyalties within the human heart. A holy city can become an image of the life ordered toward God.

We already think this way in ordinary experience. We speak of someone being "lost" without referring to geography. We speak of "finding ourselves" without consulting a map. We describe seasons of growth, exile, struggle, and homecoming. Physical realities become language for inward realities because the visible world often mirrors the invisible one.

This way of reading does not erase history. It deepens it. The stories matter not only because they happened but because they reveal patterns that continue to unfold within every generation and every person. The outward narrative becomes a window into universal truths about human nature and our relationship with God.

When we begin reading this way, scripture changes from a collection of ancient records into a living conversation. The names and places still belong to a distant time, yet they also begin to describe experiences that are surprisingly familiar. The stories cease to be merely about people long ago and start speaking about the movements of the soul itself.

This is why careful reading matters. The deeper meaning is not usually handed to us immediately. It must be sought with patience, reflection, and humility. But once we begin to perceive these patterns, the landscape of scripture opens before us in a new way. The historical story remains, yet it becomes something more—a map that helps us understand not only where others have traveled, but where we ourselves are being led.
