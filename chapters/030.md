## 30 — The Same God

> *"The Savior knows no other Father except God the founder and creator of all things."*

Sometimes people divide God into two different pictures. On one side stands the God of creation, law, judgment, and history. On the other stands the God revealed through Jesus, characterized by mercy, forgiveness, and love. The tension between these pictures has led many to wonder whether they can truly belong to the same source.

The question is not new. It was already being debated in the early centuries of Christianity. Some argued that the God who spoke through Moses and the prophets was a lesser being, while the Father revealed by Jesus was a higher and better God. If this were true, the Old Testament and the Gospel would tell two different stories about two different divine beings.

The argument presented here begins with a simple observation: Jesus consistently speaks as though the God of Israel is his Father. When he quotes the prophets, he treats them as trustworthy witnesses. When he refers to the temple, he calls it his Father's house. When he teaches people to pray, he directs them toward the Creator who sends rain on the just and the unjust and causes the sun to shine on both good and evil alike. Everywhere the same pattern appears. Jesus does not distance himself from the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. He identifies himself with that God.

The reasoning becomes even stronger when we consider what Jesus admired. Again and again he points people back to the commandments to love God and love neighbor. He praises the wisdom found in the Scriptures of Israel. He appeals to the words of Moses and the prophets as sources of truth rather than as errors to be corrected. If he believed the God behind those writings was someone other than his Father, his actions would make little sense. He would constantly be drawing distinctions. Instead, he repeatedly builds bridges.

At a deeper level, the argument touches on the nature of divine character. If there were two gods—one responsible for creation and another responsible for redemption—then reality itself would be divided. The world would have one source, while salvation would come from another. Creation and love would belong to different hands. Yet the entire message of the Gospel points in the opposite direction. The one who made the world is also the one who seeks to heal it. The one who formed humanity is also the one who desires its restoration.

This insight has practical consequences. It means that the story of Scripture is not a tale of competing gods but a single unfolding purpose. The God who called Abraham, guided Israel, spoke through the prophets, and created the heavens and earth is the same God revealed in Christ. The methods may differ from age to age. The lessons may take different forms. But the heart behind them remains unchanged.

Seen this way, the Bible becomes one continuous story rather than two disconnected books joined together. Creation and redemption belong to the same author. The hand that fashioned the world is the same hand that reaches out to heal it. And the love revealed in Christ is not a departure from the divine purpose but its clearest expression.
