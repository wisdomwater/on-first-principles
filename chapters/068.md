## 68 — The Gift and the Choice

> *"To will and to work are of God."*

At first glance, this statement seems to create a serious problem. If both our willing and our working come from God, then what remains for us to do? If every desire and every action ultimately comes from God, it might seem that human freedom disappears altogether. We become instruments moved by forces beyond ourselves, deserving neither praise nor blame.

Yet that is not the conclusion being drawn here. The key lies in understanding the difference between possessing a power and deciding how to use it. Consider something as simple as movement. The ability to move your hands, speak words, or walk from one place to another is a gift. You did not create the muscles, nerves, or life that make movement possible. Yet the existence of that power does not determine how you will use it. The same hand can offer help or inflict harm. The same voice can encourage or deceive.

In this sense, the power itself comes from God, while the direction of that power remains our responsibility. We have been given the capacity to think, choose, desire, and act. These are gifts woven into our nature. But gifts are not destinies. They provide possibilities, not outcomes. What we do with those possibilities is where freedom enters the picture.

A useful comparison can be found in the gift of speech. The ability to speak is something we receive. No one teaches an infant how to create vocal cords or breath. Yet once speech develops, a person may use it for truth or falsehood, kindness or cruelty, wisdom or foolishness. We do not say that every lie comes from God because the power of speech comes from God. Rather, we recognize that the gift is good while its use depends upon the one who possesses it.

The same principle applies to willing and working. The capacity to desire, choose, and act belongs to the structure of human life itself. Without that capacity we could do nothing at all. Yet the existence of a power does not determine the direction in which it is exercised. We can bend our desires toward selfishness or generosity, toward truth or deception, toward love or indifference. The power is given; the choice is ours.

This understanding also preserves the meaning of moral responsibility. If every good or evil action were caused directly by God, praise and blame would lose their meaning. Commands, warnings, encouragements, and corrections would become pointless. The entire moral life depends upon the reality that human beings participate in their own formation. We are not merely acted upon; we act.

At the same time, recognizing that our powers are gifts cultivates humility. We cannot boast as though we created ourselves. Every ability we possess rests upon foundations we did not build. The power to think, to love, to choose, to create, and even to seek God arises from a life we first received. Freedom does not begin with self-creation. It begins with a gift. What matters is how we respond to that gift and what we allow it to become in our hands.
