## 18 — The End Returns to the Beginning

> *“The goodness of God through Christ will restore his entire creation to one end.”*

When people think about the end of the story, they often imagine a final division. Some are saved. Others are lost. The story closes, the verdict is announced, and everything remains fixed forever.

But consider another possibility.

What if the purpose of God's judgments is not simply to separate, but to heal? What if divine correction exists not to preserve division, but to overcome it? What if the end toward which creation moves is not endless fragmentation, but restored harmony?

This is the question before us.

Scripture says that Christ must reign until all enemies are placed beneath his feet and all things are subjected to him. Yet what does that mean? If subjection means destruction alone, then Christ's victory would consist merely in overpowering his opponents. But when we ourselves speak of being subject to Christ, we mean something very different. We mean surrendering ourselves to the source of life. We mean entering into salvation. We mean becoming whole.

The same word points toward a deeper mystery. Perhaps Christ conquers not by annihilating what opposes him, but by healing it. Perhaps his victory is so complete that nothing remains outside the reach of his transforming goodness.

To understand this, we must look back to the beginning.

Every rational creature came from a single source. Yet we do not all stand in the same condition now. Some shine with wisdom. Some struggle in confusion. Some seem far removed from the life for which they were created. How did such differences arise? Not because God created different kinds of souls, some noble and some corrupt. Rather, each being moved according to the freedom that had been given to it. Some remained close to the good. Others drifted from it to varying degrees.

This explains both the diversity of creation and the justice of God. We experience different conditions because we have made different uses of our freedom. Yet none of these conditions represent our final destination.

The end reveals the purpose hidden within the beginning.

If all things came from one source, and if God remains perfectly good, then the final goal of creation cannot be endless division. The movement of providence must ultimately be toward restoration. What was scattered must be gathered. What was wounded must be healed. What departed from the good must somehow be brought back into harmony with it.

This does not mean that evil is ignored. On the contrary, every soul must face the consequences of its choices. Nothing false survives God's refining work. Every distortion must be exposed. Every attachment to evil must be surrendered. Judgment remains real and necessary.

Yet judgment is not the final word.

Beyond correction stands restoration. Beyond wandering stands return. Beyond exile stands homecoming.

The beginning and the end mirror one another. We came forth from God, diversified by the movements of freedom and history. The work of Christ is to draw creation back toward unity, not by force, but through the patient triumph of truth, goodness, and love.

In that final harmony, the purpose of creation will at last be fully revealed: not merely that God made the world, but that nothing good which comes from God is abandoned by him.
