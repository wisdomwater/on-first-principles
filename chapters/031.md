## 31 — Seeing with the Mind

> *“He who has seen the Son has seen the Father also.”*

One of the oldest questions people ask about God is surprisingly simple: If God is real, why can’t we see him?

At first glance, this seems like a powerful objection. Scripture contains stories of Moses speaking with God and prophets receiving divine visions. Yet elsewhere we are told that no one has seen God. How can both be true?

The difficulty begins when we assume that knowing and seeing are the same thing. We naturally think that what is most real should be visible. We trust our eyes. We believe that if something cannot be pointed to, measured, or observed, then it must be less certain than the things around us. But consider some of the most important realities in your life. You cannot see justice. You cannot hold wisdom in your hand. You cannot look directly at love, truth, or understanding. Yet no one doubts that these things exist.

Perhaps God belongs to this deeper category of reality.

If God were merely another object in the universe, then God could be seen the way a mountain or a tree is seen. But anything visible has shape, size, boundaries, and location. It occupies space. It can be measured. It belongs to the world of material things. Yet the source of all things cannot simply be one more thing among the things it created.

This changes how we understand passages that speak of seeing God. When Moses encounters God, the point is not that he looked upon a physical form with his eyes. Rather, he came to know something of God's nature. His vision was a vision of understanding. The encounter happened within the mind and heart rather than through ordinary sight.

The same principle helps explain Jesus' words: “He who has seen the Son has seen the Father.” This is not a statement about physical resemblance. It is a statement about revelation. To truly understand the Son is to understand the Father whom the Son reveals. Just as understanding a great work of art can reveal the mind of the artist, understanding Christ reveals the character and nature of God.

This distinction between seeing and knowing matters more than it may first appear. Much spiritual confusion comes from looking for God in the wrong way. We search for something dramatic, visible, and undeniable. We expect certainty to arrive through the senses. Yet the deepest truths often reveal themselves through understanding rather than observation. We come to know them gradually, through insight, reflection, and experience.

The eye can perceive only surfaces. The mind can perceive meaning.

When the language of Scripture speaks of seeing God, it invites us beyond the world of appearances and into the world of understanding. The goal is not merely to look at God, but to know God. And in that deeper kind of seeing, the vision of the heart reaches farther than the eyes ever could.
