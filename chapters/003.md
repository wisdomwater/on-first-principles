## 3 — Looking Beyond the Surface

> *"God is light, and in him is no darkness at all."*

One of Origen's favorite questions is deceptively simple: when Scripture says something about God, what does it really mean?

In this passage he considers several biblical descriptions of God. Scripture calls God a consuming fire. It calls God spirit. It calls God light. Some people in Origen's day took these descriptions very literally and imagined that God must somehow be made of fire, or spirit, or some kind of heavenly substance. Origen believed this approach missed the deeper point. These words, he argues, are not trying to tell us what God is physically made of. They are attempting to point us toward realities that lie beyond ordinary human language.

Consider the image of light. When John writes that God is light, few people imagine that God is a giant sun shining somewhere in the universe. Light is being used as a symbol for something we already associate with light itself: illumination, understanding, clarity, and truth. Just as sunlight allows our eyes to see the world around us, God's presence allows the mind and heart to perceive reality more clearly. We see what was hidden. We understand what was confusing. We recognize what is true.

Origen applies the same reasoning to the image of fire. If God is a consuming fire, what exactly is being consumed? Surely not wood, grass, or physical objects. Rather, he suggests, the divine fire burns away what does not belong in us. Pride, selfishness, resentment, destructive desires—these are the things consumed when a person genuinely encounters God. The fire of God is not primarily a force of destruction. It is a force of purification.

This leads Origen toward one of his most important ideas: Scripture often contains depths that are not immediately visible. A passage may have an obvious meaning, but it may also point toward something larger. Reading Scripture is therefore not unlike walking through a landscape at dawn. At first only the outlines are visible. As the light increases, details begin to emerge that were present all along but hidden from view.

The same principle appears in his discussion of the Holy Spirit. Origen argues that the Spirit should not be imagined as some material substance divided into pieces and distributed among believers. Instead, he compares the Spirit to something like wisdom or knowledge. Many people can share in wisdom without wisdom being cut into fragments. In the same way, many people can participate in the life of the Spirit without diminishing it. The Spirit is not a thing to possess but a reality in which we participate.

Beneath all of these examples lies a larger lesson. Human language is wonderfully useful, but it has limits. When speaking about ultimate realities, words become signposts rather than photographs. They point beyond themselves. Fire points toward purification. Light points toward truth. Spirit points toward a reality not confined by material limitations. Origen invites his readers to look through the words rather than merely at them.

This invitation remains surprisingly relevant today. We often assume that understanding means arriving at a neat definition. Origen reminds us that some truths are better approached through images, symbols, and lived experience. The deepest realities may not fit comfortably inside our descriptions. Sometimes wisdom begins when we stop asking only, "What does this word mean?" and start asking, "What reality is this word pointing toward?"

**Thought to Ponder**

*Are there places in your life where you have been looking only at the surface of things, when a deeper meaning may be waiting to be discovered?*
