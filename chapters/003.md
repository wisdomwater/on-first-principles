## 3 — Beyond the Images

> *“God is light, and in him is no darkness at all.”*

When we speak about God, we almost always borrow words from the physical world. Scripture calls God a light, a fire, a spirit, a rock, a shepherd, a king, and a father. Yet a question immediately arises: are these descriptions meant to tell us what God literally is, or are they helping us understand something deeper?

Consider the image of light. No one imagines that God is a giant sun shining somewhere in the universe. We understand instinctively that the word points beyond itself. Light allows us to see. It reveals what would otherwise remain hidden. In the same way, God illuminates the mind and heart. When confusion gives way to understanding, when truth suddenly becomes clear, we experience something of this divine light. The image works not because God resembles sunlight, but because sunlight resembles a deeper reality already present in God.

The same principle applies to the image of fire. Scripture calls God a consuming fire, but what does God consume? Surely not wood, grass, or physical objects. The image becomes far more meaningful when we recognize that there are other things in need of burning away. Pride, bitterness, greed, hatred, and destructive desires cling to the soul much like dead branches cling to a tree. The fire of God is the power that purifies. It destroys what diminishes life so that something healthier and more beautiful can emerge in its place.

This way of reading Scripture requires us to look beneath the surface. Words are important, but they are not the destination. They are signposts. A traveler who becomes fascinated with the sign itself may never reach the city toward which it points. In the same way, spiritual growth involves learning to see through the image to the reality behind it. What matters is not merely reading the words but discovering what they reveal.

This also helps explain why spiritual truths can seem hidden. Two people may read the same passage and come away with very different understandings. One sees only the outer form; the other begins to perceive the deeper meaning. The difference is not intelligence but attentiveness. As understanding grows, familiar words open into larger realities. What once seemed simple begins to disclose layers of wisdom that were present all along.

The same principle extends to the Holy Spirit. The Spirit is not a substance divided into pieces and distributed among people. Think of a skill such as music. Thousands may participate in music, yet music itself is not sliced into fragments and handed out. Each person shares in it according to their capacity. In a far deeper way, people share in the life of the Spirit. The Spirit is not diminished by being present in many lives at once. Rather, the Spirit is a living divine presence that awakens, sanctifies, and transforms all who become receptive to it.

Learning to read Scripture this way changes how we approach faith. The goal is not to imagine God as a larger version of something we already know. It is to allow familiar images to stretch our understanding toward realities greater than anything our senses can grasp. The images remain valuable, but only when they lead us beyond themselves.
