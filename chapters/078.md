## 78 — Why Are We Divided Within Ourselves?

> *"The flesh lusteth against the spirit, and the spirit against the flesh."*

Most people have experienced the strange feeling of wanting two different things at the same time.

Part of us wants to be patient, yet another part wants to lash out. Part of us wants to live wisely, yet another part seeks immediate satisfaction. We make resolutions and then break them. We recognize what is right and yet find ourselves pulled in another direction. At times it can feel as though there are two people living within us.

This experience raises an important question. What exactly is happening inside us when these competing desires arise?

Some have concluded that there must be two souls within a human being. One soul is higher, spiritual, and drawn toward God. The other is lower, earthly, and responsible for our selfish impulses. According to this view, the conflict we feel is a conflict between these two distinct principles struggling for control.

Others have proposed a different explanation. Perhaps there is only one soul, but it finds itself joined to a body with its own needs, appetites, and instincts. Hunger, fatigue, desire, fear, and countless other impulses continually press themselves upon our attention. The conflict, then, is not between two souls but between higher aspirations and more immediate desires.

Still others have suggested that the soul itself contains different capacities. Reason seeks what is true and good. The emotions seek what is pleasurable or avoid what is painful. Much of life becomes an effort to bring these different movements into harmony rather than allowing them to pull us apart.

Whatever explanation one prefers, the experience itself is undeniable. Human beings are not simple creatures. We are capable of remarkable generosity and surprising selfishness, profound wisdom and obvious foolishness, often within the same day. The struggle is not merely between ourselves and the world around us. It also takes place within the landscape of our own hearts.

This is why spiritual growth requires self-understanding. If we imagine ourselves to be completely unified, we will be confused by our own contradictions. But once we recognize the different desires, fears, habits, and aspirations that operate within us, we begin to understand why growth is often slow and why inner transformation requires patience.

The goal is not to wage war against part of ourselves. Nor is it to pretend that every impulse deserves equal authority. The task is to learn which movements lead toward life and which lead away from it. Wisdom begins when we stop being surprised by the conflict and start learning how to navigate it.

For before we can overcome the divisions within us, we must first acknowledge that they exist.
