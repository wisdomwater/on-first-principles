## 25 — The End That Becomes a Beginning

> *"The end of this world is the beginning of the world to come."*

When we think about endings, we often imagine finality. A book reaches its last page. A journey arrives at its destination. A chapter closes and the story is over. But what if the deepest patterns of reality do not work that way? What if every ending is also the doorway to a new beginning?

If the world arose through the many choices and movements of free creatures, then it follows that the story cannot simply stop. The diversity we see around us—the different stages of growth, wisdom, struggle, and maturity among souls—will not suddenly disappear at the close of an age. Whatever condition creatures have reached by the end of one world becomes the starting point for whatever comes next. The conclusion of one chapter becomes the opening sentence of another.

This means that creation is not static. It is a living process. Souls are always moving, learning, growing, resisting, returning, advancing, or wandering. God does not abandon this process but continually works within it, guiding all things toward restoration. The universe is not a machine wound up and left alone. It is more like a vast work of cultivation, where growth unfolds across seasons too large for us to measure.

To explain how such a process can continue, the discussion turns to the nature of matter itself. Look closely at the world and you will notice that physical things are constantly changing form. Wood becomes flame. Flame becomes smoke. Food becomes flesh and bone. Water evaporates into air and returns again as rain. Beneath all these transformations lies a deeper reality: matter is capable of taking on many forms. It is flexible, adaptable, and responsive.

This observation leads to a larger question. Where did such a remarkable capacity come from? Some philosophers argued that matter had always existed alongside God, as though it were an eternal raw material waiting to be shaped. But that explanation raises more questions than it answers. If matter perfectly serves the purposes of creation, if it possesses exactly the qualities needed for the world that exists, why assume it appeared by chance? Would it not make more sense to see it as part of the wisdom of the Creator rather than something independent of him?

The argument ultimately points toward a vision of radical dependence upon God. Not only the forms of creation, but the very substance from which those forms arise, owes its existence to the divine source. The world is not built from materials that stand outside God's care and power. Everything rests upon that creative generosity. Matter changes. Worlds begin and end. Ages rise and pass away. Yet through all those transformations, the sustaining presence of God remains.

Seen this way, history becomes less like a line with a finish point and more like a river flowing toward the sea. Every ending carries something forward. Every conclusion prepares the ground for what comes next. And beneath the countless changes of the world stands the same creative wisdom, patiently bringing all things toward their fulfillment.
