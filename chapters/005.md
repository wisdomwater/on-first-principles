## 5 — Knowing the Invisible

> *“Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God.”*

If God cannot be seen with physical eyes, how can anyone ever know him?

That question naturally follows from everything we have considered so far. If God is not a body, does not occupy a place, and cannot be captured by images, then what kind of relationship can we possibly have with him? It might seem as though God becomes more distant the more we remove these familiar ways of thinking. Yet the opposite is true. As we let go of inadequate images, we begin to discover a deeper way of knowing.

Consider your own mind for a moment. You can see your hands and hear your voice, but can you see a thought? Can you weigh a memory? Can you measure an idea with a ruler? The most important activities of the mind take place in a realm that is invisible. We grasp mathematical truths, contemplate justice, remember the past, imagine the future, and wrestle with questions of meaning. None of these realities can be touched or held in the way we handle physical objects, yet no one doubts that they are real.

This observation becomes the foundation of a larger argument. If the human mind can know realities that are not material, then perhaps reality itself is larger than the physical world alone. The mind seems uniquely fitted to engage with truths that transcend matter. It can reach beyond what the eyes see and the hands touch. It can contemplate beauty, truth, goodness, and wisdom. There is something remarkable about a creature made of flesh and blood that can nevertheless concern itself with eternal things.

For this reason, there is a kind of kinship between the human mind and the divine. The mind is not God, nor does it comprehend God fully. Yet it bears a likeness that allows it to recognize traces of the divine when it encounters them. Just as the eye is naturally suited for light, the mind is naturally suited for truth. When the mind is clouded by distraction, selfishness, or confusion, its vision becomes dim. When it is purified and clarified, it becomes more capable of perceiving realities that were always present but previously unseen.

This helps explain a puzzling statement found throughout Scripture: that people may "see" God. Such language is not referring to physical sight. God is not an object standing somewhere in the universe waiting to be observed. Rather, to see God is to know God. It is an act of understanding, recognition, and participation. We speak this way even in ordinary life. When someone finally understands a difficult problem, they often say, "I see." What they mean is not that their eyes have perceived something new, but that their mind has grasped a truth.

When Jesus says that the pure in heart shall see God, the heart should be understood as the center of understanding and perception. A purified heart is a clarified mind, free enough from distortion to recognize what is real. The promise is not that God will suddenly become visible in a physical sense. The promise is that those who are transformed inwardly will become capable of a deeper knowledge of the One who has always been beyond the reach of the senses.

The path to knowing God, then, is not primarily a journey outward but inward. It is the gradual refinement of our capacity to recognize truth. As the mind grows clearer and the heart grows purer, it becomes possible to perceive more and more of the divine reality that surrounds and sustains all things, even though it can never be fully contained within our understanding.
