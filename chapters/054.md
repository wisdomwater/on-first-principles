## 54 — The Power of Assent

> *“To judge and determine how we ought to use them when they come is the work and business of that reason which is in us.”*

Every day countless impressions arrive at the door of your mind. A tempting opportunity appears. An insult is spoken. A desire arises. A fear takes hold. A pleasant image catches your attention. Before you have chosen anything at all, the world is already presenting possibilities and provoking reactions within you.

This much is not under your control.

You cannot entirely prevent thoughts from appearing, desires from awakening, or circumstances from confronting you. Just as your eyes receive light and your ears receive sound, your mind continually receives impressions from the world around it. Some attract you. Others repel you. Some stir noble impulses, while others awaken impulses you would rather not have. The mere appearance of these inner movements is not yet a moral choice.

This is where human beings differ from creatures driven only by instinct. A spider spins its web because its nature directs it to do so. A bee builds its hive because it follows patterns woven into its being. Human beings also possess instincts and desires, but they possess something more as well: the ability to examine what arises within them. We can stand back from our impulses and ask whether they deserve our cooperation.

For this reason, an impulse is not the same thing as an action. Desire may knock at the door, but it cannot enter without permission. Imagine a judge listening to arguments presented by opposing sides. Each side makes its case. Each seeks approval. Yet the arguments themselves do not determine the verdict. The decision belongs to the judge. In much the same way, desires, fears, attractions, and temptations present themselves before the court of reason. They make their appeal, but they do not possess the authority to pronounce judgment.

Consider someone who has committed himself to honesty. One day a lie presents an easy escape from embarrassment. The opportunity appears. The temptation is real. Yet the temptation itself has not forced anything to happen. Between the appearance of the opportunity and the spoken lie lies a moment of assent. The person may approve the impulse or reject it. The possibility arrives from outside, but the consent comes from within.

This is why praise and blame remain meaningful. If every action were simply the inevitable result of external pressures or internal urges, responsibility would disappear. We would be no more accountable than a river flowing downhill. But human beings possess the remarkable capacity to evaluate their impulses rather than merely obey them. We can strengthen a good desire or resist a destructive one. We can cultivate habits that support wisdom or surrender to habits that diminish us. Freedom does not mean that we choose every thought that enters our minds. It means that we retain the power to decide which thoughts, desires, and impulses will be allowed to shape the person we become.
