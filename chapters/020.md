## 20 — Are the Stars Alive?

> *“The stars also are not clean in his sight.”*

We often look at the heavens and imagine permanence.

The sun rises every morning. The moon follows its course. The stars appear fixed in their appointed places. Their movements seem so regular, so dependable, that it is natural to think of them as fundamentally different from ourselves. We change. They endure.

But should we assume that what appears constant is therefore incapable of change?

This question leads us into a fascinating lines of inquiry. We have already concluded that every created being exists because God created it, and that everything created possesses freedom. Goodness belongs essentially only to God. Every creature participates in goodness, but no creature possesses it by nature in the same way that God does.

If this is true, then we must ask whether there are exceptions.

Are the sun, moon, and stars somehow beyond change? Are they incapable of turning from the good? Are they fixed forever in their present condition? Some people have thought so. Yet such a view creates difficulties.

For if any creature is incapable of choosing otherwise, then its goodness ceases to be meaningful. We do not praise a stone for remaining a stone. We do not admire a machine for doing only what it was designed to do. Praise belongs where freedom exists.

This leads to a startling possibility. Perhaps the heavenly bodies are not merely objects but participants in God's creation in a deeper sense than we ordinarily imagine.

Notice what prompts the question. Scripture says that even the stars are "not clean" in God's sight. Whatever else that statement means, it suggests that the stars are not being measured merely as physical objects. Dust on a star would hardly be a moral concern. The language seems to point toward something more profound than physical brightness or dimness.

So we are invited to ask whether the heavenly bodies possess some form of life, awareness, or rational nature. Could they be more than glowing objects suspended in the sky? Could they participate in the vast drama of creation alongside every other rational creature?

We cannot claim certainty here. The question exceeds our present knowledge. Yet it serves an important purpose.

The deeper issue is whether any created thing stands outside the great law we have been tracing throughout this work: that all creatures come from God, all receive their gifts from God, and all remain dependent upon God. No created being, however glorious, possesses perfection as its own possession.

The stars themselves become a lesson in humility.

When we look upward, we are tempted to see permanence and self-sufficiency. Yet the heavens, no less than ourselves, belong to a created order. Their splendor points beyond itself. Their beauty is borrowed beauty. Their light is received light.

And perhaps that is true of every excellence we admire.

The brightest star shines only because it participates in a greater light. The wisest soul possesses wisdom only because wisdom has first been given. The highest creature remains a creature still.

To recognize this is not to diminish the heavens. It is to see them rightly. Their glory lies not in independence, but in their continual participation in the goodness from which all things come.
