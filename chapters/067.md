## 67 — Effort and Grace

> *"It is not of him that willeth, nor of him that runneth, but of God that hath mercy."*

At first hearing, these words seem to leave little room for human effort. If salvation depends on God's mercy and not on our willing or striving, then what role do our choices play? Why struggle, learn, persevere, or seek what is good if everything ultimately depends on God?

Before answering, it helps to ask a simpler question. Is it a good thing to desire what is good? Is it praiseworthy when someone pursues wisdom, kindness, truth, or virtue? Most people would immediately answer yes. We naturally admire those who devote themselves to worthy goals. Their effort matters. Their choices matter. Their determination matters.

Yet we also know that effort alone is not enough. A builder may work tirelessly, but if the foundation fails, the structure collapses. A farmer may plant and water faithfully, yet without sunlight, rain, and the hidden processes of life, no harvest appears. Human labor is real and necessary, but it always depends upon conditions that lie beyond human control.

This is the key to understanding the passage. The statement does not deny the value of willing and striving. Instead, it denies that willing and striving are sufficient by themselves. A person may set their heart upon what is good and run toward it with all their strength, but the final outcome still depends upon realities greater than themselves. Human effort participates in the work; it does not complete the work alone.

Think of a city under siege. The watchmen stand their posts. The defenders remain vigilant. The soldiers fight bravely. If the city survives, no sensible person would say their efforts were meaningless. Yet neither would they claim the victory belonged entirely to them. Countless factors beyond their control contributed to the outcome. Gratitude naturally extends beyond the defenders to the larger providence that preserved them.

Or consider a ship crossing a dangerous sea. The sailors labor, the navigator studies the stars, and the captain guides the vessel through storms and currents. Yet even with all their skill, they do not command the winds or calm the waves. When the ship finally reaches harbor, they know their expertise played a role, but they also know they were aided by forces beyond themselves. Their success was neither entirely their own nor entirely apart from their efforts.

This is how spiritual growth works. We are not passive spectators waiting for God to do everything, nor are we solitary achievers capable of perfecting ourselves through sheer determination. We choose, strive, learn, repent, and persevere. Yet beneath all of those efforts lies a deeper source of strength, opportunity, wisdom, and grace. The greater part of the work belongs to God, but that does not make our part insignificant. Rather, it gives our efforts their proper place. We labor, yet we do so within a reality sustained by gifts we did not create and could never provide for ourselves. The more clearly we see this partnership between effort and grace, the more both humility and gratitude begin to grow.
