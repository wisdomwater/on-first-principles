## 90 — Treasures in Earthen Vessels

> *"We have this treasure in earthen vessels."*

One of the most common objections to Scripture is that it does not always appear impressive. Some passages are difficult. Others seem ordinary. The language is often simple rather than polished. If these writings truly contain divine wisdom, why are they not more obviously magnificent?

To answer this question, consider the world itself. If there is a divine wisdom guiding creation, it is not always immediately visible. Some aspects of reality reveal their order quite clearly. The movements of the stars, the structure of a living creature, or the patterns of nature often inspire wonder. Yet other things seem confusing. Human suffering, unexpected events, and the tangled complexities of history can leave us struggling to discern any larger purpose. The fact that we do not fully understand providence does not prove providence is absent. It may simply reveal the limits of our vision.

The same principle applies to Scripture. A person may encounter a passage whose meaning is not immediately clear and conclude that nothing profound is present. But that conclusion may be premature. Just as the workings of nature often reveal their elegance only after careful study, the deeper wisdom of sacred writings frequently discloses itself gradually. What first appears ordinary may conceal unexpected depth.

This leads to one of the most beautiful images in the discussion: divine treasure hidden in humble containers. The wisdom of God is compared to a treasure stored in earthen vessels. The vessel itself may appear plain. It may even seem fragile. Yet its value lies not in its outward appearance but in what it contains. If the Scriptures dazzled readers primarily through literary brilliance, philosophical sophistication, or rhetorical power, people might be tempted to credit human genius rather than divine wisdom. The simplicity of the vessel directs attention toward the treasure.

This perspective also helps explain why faith does not rest solely on intellectual arguments. Certainly there are reasons to believe. There are fulfilled prophecies, historical developments, personal experiences, and the enduring influence of the biblical message. Yet beyond all of these lies something more difficult to define. Many readers find themselves gradually transformed through their encounter with these writings. The words begin to illuminate aspects of reality they had not previously seen. What once seemed obscure becomes meaningful. What once appeared ordinary begins to reveal hidden depth.

The process requires patience. The deepest truths are rarely absorbed all at once. A child begins with simple lessons before advancing to more complex understanding. In the same way, spiritual wisdom unfolds in stages. We often start with elementary insights and gradually grow into a fuller vision. The purpose of Scripture is not merely to provide information but to guide the soul toward maturity.

For this reason, the invitation is not simply to admire sacred writings from a distance or to judge them by their surface appearance. It is to enter them deeply enough that their hidden wisdom can emerge. Divine truth often comes clothed in ordinary language, just as profound realities are often hidden within ordinary life. The treasure is there, but discovering it requires the willingness to keep digging. As understanding deepens, what once seemed like simple words may become a doorway into mysteries that extend far beyond the reach of human wisdom alone.
