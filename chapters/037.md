## 37 — The Fire and Its Warmth

> *“The divine fire itself rested in him, and from this some warmth has come to all others.”*

When a fire burns in a hearth, everyone nearby can feel its warmth.

Those standing closest experience its heat most strongly. Those farther away feel it less. Yet all receive something from the same source.

This image helps illuminate what has been said about Christ's union with God.

If Christ is like iron completely permeated by fire, then the prophets, apostles, and saints are like those who stand near that blazing heat. They share in its light. They receive its warmth. They are transformed by its presence. Yet there remains a difference between possessing the fire itself and participating in its radiance.

This distinction matters because it preserves two truths at once.

On the one hand, the saints are genuinely united with God. They are not merely spectators. The divine life touches them, changes them, and draws them into communion. Every act of wisdom, love, courage, and holiness is a participation in something greater than themselves.

On the other hand, Christ's union with God is unique. The prophets received inspiration. The apostles received grace. The saints received gifts according to their capacity. But in Christ there is not merely a share of divine life. The fullness itself is present.

Imagine a vessel filled with fragrant oil. Those nearby carry its scent on their clothing. The fragrance clings to them and spreads wherever they go. Yet the fragrance they bear is not the same as the vessel that contains the oil itself. They participate in what the vessel possesses.

In the same way, every holy person bears something of Christ's life. The more closely one draws near to him, the more his character begins to shape one's own. Yet Christ remains the source from which all such transformation flows.

There is an encouraging implication hidden here.

The distance between Christ and humanity is not meant to leave us discouraged. The point is not that he possesses what we can never approach. Rather, his life reveals what becomes possible when a human being is entirely open to God. The warmth that radiates from him is not locked away. It is continually shared.

This is why spiritual growth is often described in terms of participation rather than achievement. We do not manufacture holiness by our own efforts. We move closer to the fire. We remain near the source. We allow ourselves to be warmed, illuminated, and gradually transformed.

Origen's imagery of shadows points in the same direction. A shadow follows the movements of the reality that casts it. So too, the life of Christ reveals the movements of God within human existence. In him, the invisible becomes visible. The divine life takes on a human form that can be seen, followed, and imitated.

Yet even these images ultimately reach their limits.

Again and again, Origen reminds us that what we see now is only a partial glimpse. We understand through symbols, comparisons, and reflections. We perceive the warmth, but not yet the full fire. We see the shadow, but not yet the complete reality.

For now, that is enough.

The warmth itself tells us that the fire exists. And every act of truth, love, and holiness is a sign that its light is already reaching the world.
