## 13 — Growing Into What You Were Made To Be

> *“There are diversities of gifts, but the same Spirit.”*

What does spiritual growth actually look like?

Many people imagine it as acquiring more knowledge, becoming more disciplined, or learning religious practices. While those things may help, the deeper reality is more organic. Growth is the gradual unfolding of what was planted within us from the beginning. It is the movement from mere existence toward fullness of life.

The passage before us presents a remarkable vision of that journey. First comes existence itself. Every creature receives being from God. The gift of existence is universal, extending to everything that lives. Then comes reason, the capacity to understand, discern, and choose. Through the divine Word, human beings are given the ability to recognize truth and to distinguish between good and evil. Yet even this is not the end of the story.

A person may exist and possess intelligence while still remaining spiritually asleep. One may know many things and yet remain trapped by selfishness, fear, pride, or confusion. Something more is needed. This is where the work of the Holy Spirit enters. The Spirit is not simply concerned with making us informed; the Spirit is concerned with making us holy. Wherever a person begins to turn away from what diminishes life and toward what heals and restores it, the Spirit is already at work.

The old self must be put aside so that something new can emerge. This does not mean becoming a different person. It means becoming the person one was always meant to be. The image of new wine poured into new wineskins captures this beautifully. Transformation requires more than adding a few improvements to an old way of living. The vessel itself must be renewed. The soul becomes capable of receiving greater gifts only as it becomes capable of holding them.

This helps explain a difficult saying of Jesus about blasphemy against the Holy Spirit. It not as a single forbidden phrase but as a tragic reversal. To receive the Spirit's transforming work and then deliberately turn away from it is more serious than simple ignorance. A person who has not yet seen may stumble in darkness. But a person who has seen the path and knowingly abandons it rejects the very power that was leading them toward life.

Yet the overall movement of the passage is hopeful rather than fearful. The spiritual life is a series of enlargements. Existence leads to reason. Reason leads to holiness. Holiness opens the way to wisdom. Wisdom deepens into knowledge of God. At each stage the soul becomes more capable of receiving what comes next. Growth is not a ladder climbed by human effort alone but a gift continually offered and continually received.

The final goal is strikingly simple. God desires that what he created may become fully what it was intended to be. A seed becomes a tree. A child becomes an adult. In the same way, the soul is meant to grow into the likeness of its source. The Father's gift of being, the Son's gift of understanding, and the Spirit's gift of sanctification are not separate blessings but stages of a single movement. Together they lead the soul toward wholeness, until the life given by God becomes a life increasingly capable of reflecting the goodness, wisdom, and love from which it came.
