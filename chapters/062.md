## 62 — The Patient Farmer

> *"The great Farmer of all nature delays the benefit which we should consider quick, for fear lest it should prove superficial."*

Most of us want growth to happen quickly. We want understanding without confusion, wisdom without mistakes, and maturity without struggle. When we see someone flourishing, we naturally wonder why the same progress is not happening in ourselves or in others. Why does transformation seem to come swiftly for some and slowly for others?

Consider a skilled farmer. Not every field is planted in exactly the same way or at exactly the same time. Soil differs from place to place. One patch of ground may be ready immediately, while another requires preparation before seed can take root. A careless observer might accuse the farmer of neglect, but the farmer sees something deeper. Seed that sprouts too quickly in shallow soil may produce an impressive beginning, yet wither when heat and drought arrive. A slower growth can sometimes produce stronger roots.

The same principle applies to the growth of the soul. There are times when we desire spiritual insight, understanding, or change before we are prepared to sustain it. We want the harvest without the cultivation. Yet some lessons can only be learned through experience. Sometimes a person must discover the consequences of impatience before becoming ready for true wisdom. What appears to be delay may actually be preparation.

This helps explain a difficult question: why do some people seem to receive truth and then quickly abandon it? The answer offered here is not that the effort was wasted. Even failure can teach something valuable. A person who rushes ahead and later stumbles may gain a clearer understanding of their own condition. They learn the limits of shallow enthusiasm. They discover that lasting transformation requires more than momentary excitement. In that painful recognition, genuine growth can begin.

Underlying all of this is the conviction that human beings are endlessly varied. No two souls travel exactly the same path. Each person carries a unique mixture of strengths, weaknesses, desires, fears, and experiences. Because of this, no single method can heal everyone. What encourages one person may discourage another. What needs to happen immediately for one soul may need to wait years for another. The wisdom required to guide such complexity belongs ultimately to God alone.

This perspective invites patience. We often judge a story long before it is finished. We see only a single season of growth and assume we know the outcome. Yet the gardener sees the entire field, the changing weather, the condition of the roots, and the harvest still to come. What looks like delay may be wisdom. What appears to be failure may become preparation. And what seems unfinished today may simply be part of a much longer work whose purpose is not rapid growth, but lasting transformation.
