## 16 — No One Is Good by Nature Alone

> *“The position of every created being is the result of his own work and his own motives.”*

Why are some beings exalted while others are fallen?

It is a question that naturally arises whenever we encounter the variety of existence. Some appear wise and holy. Others seem trapped in ignorance or rebellion. Scripture itself speaks of angels, powers, dominions, rulers, and hostile spiritual forces. Faced with such differences, one explanation immediately suggests itself: perhaps God simply created them that way.

But follow that thought to its conclusion.

If angels are holy by nature and incapable of becoming otherwise, then their goodness is not really their own. They deserve no praise, for they could not have chosen differently. Likewise, if evil powers were created evil from the beginning, then they deserve no blame. Their wickedness would not arise from any misuse of freedom but from the way they were made. In that case, responsibility would disappear from both sides. Holiness would become a built-in feature, and corruption would become a predetermined fate.

This is impossible to accept. Praise and blame make sense only where freedom exists. We honor courage because fear was possible. We admire generosity because selfishness could have been chosen instead. Every moral judgment assumes the presence of genuine freedom.

From this principle we can draw a bold conclusion. The various ranks and conditions of rational beings cannot be explained by differences in their original nature. Angels are not holy because they were created from a better substance. Fallen powers are not corrupt because they were fashioned from inferior material. Whatever distinction exists among rational creatures must arise from the use of freedom rather than from the manner of their creation.

This idea transforms the way we think about both goodness and evil. Goodness is not a possession secured forever simply by occupying a high position. Nor is evil an unavoidable destiny. Every rational creature remains capable of movement. Advancement is possible. Decline is possible. What matters is not where one begins but how one responds to the freedom that has been given.

To support this claim, let's turns to a striking image from the prophet Ezekiel. Here we find a mysterious figure described as dwelling in paradise, adorned with beauty, walking among holy things, and remaining blameless for a time. Yet this same figure becomes proud, turns away from what is good, and falls from his exalted condition. Whatever historical meaning the passage may possess, within this is a spiritual principle: a being who was once glorious became corrupted not because corruption was part of his nature, but because of choices that gradually distorted what had once been beautiful.

The warning is profound. No created condition, however blessed, can substitute for continual faithfulness. Holiness is not something that can be stored away and forgotten. It remains alive only through participation in the good. Pride, negligence, and self-absorption slowly separate the soul from the source of its life, just as a branch withers when disconnected from the tree.

Yet hidden within the warning is a great hope. If no creature is evil by nature, then evil is not the final truth about anyone. What has been distorted can be healed. What has fallen can be raised. What has wandered can return. Freedom makes decline possible, but it also makes restoration possible.

This is one of the deepest expressions of divine justice. God does not create some for glory and others for ruin. Rather, he creates rational beings capable of participating in goodness. Their various conditions reflect the history of their choices, not the intention of their Maker. And because freedom remains, the story is never entirely finished.
