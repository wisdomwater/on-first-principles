## 45 — The Hidden History of the Soul

> *"Is there unrighteousness with God? God forbid."*

Whenever we encounter suffering or inequality, a question naturally arises: Is life fair?

Why does one person seem surrounded by opportunity while another struggles from birth? Why do some appear drawn toward goodness almost instinctively, while others face obstacles at every turn? If God is just, how can such differences exist?

This question lies at the heart of a great spiritual puzzle. If our circumstances are simply assigned without reason, then the universe appears arbitrary. Yet if some souls are created better than others, then the Creator himself would seem partial. Neither possibility is satisfying. One undermines meaning; the other undermines justice.

The solution proposed here is radical. Every rational being was created with freedom, and it was the use of that freedom that produced the differences we now observe. The diversity of creation is not rooted in unequal beginnings but in unequal responses. Some moved more fully toward the good. Others drifted away from it. The world we inhabit is the result of those countless movements being gathered together and woven into a single ordered whole.

To explain this, an image is borrowed from Scripture: a great house containing many kinds of vessels. Some are made of gold, some of silver, some of clay, some of wood. At first glance this seems unfair. Why should one vessel receive honor while another receives a humbler role? Yet the argument insists that the distinction did not originate in the potter's preference. Rather, each vessel came to occupy its place through the history of its own choices. The house contains many different vessels because freedom has produced many different outcomes.

This leads to a striking interpretation of biblical passages such as the story of Jacob and Esau. How could one be loved and another rejected before either had done good or evil? The answer offered is that the story did not begin at birth. The visible life we see is only one chapter in a much larger narrative. What appears unjust from a narrow perspective may be entirely just when viewed in the light of a history known fully only to God.

Yet this vision is not merely about reward and punishment. Running beneath it is a theme of healing. The Creator is not portrayed as a judge distributing permanent sentences. He is described as a physician arranging remedies. Every soul remains under the care of divine wisdom. Every circumstance becomes part of a larger process intended to guide creatures back toward wholeness. Even those placed in difficult conditions are not abandoned. They remain objects of God's compassion and subjects of his patient work.

What emerges is a picture of the universe as a school for freedom. Nothing is random, yet nothing is forced. Our present condition reflects previous movements of the soul, but it does not imprison us. The vessel of today is not necessarily the vessel of tomorrow. At every moment the possibility remains to be purified, transformed, and prepared for a greater purpose. Divine justice, in this view, is never separated from divine mercy. The same wisdom that orders the world is also laboring to heal it.
