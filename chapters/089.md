## 89 — More Than the Letter

> *"The advent of Christ led those who doubted to the conviction that the scriptures were composed by heavenly grace."*

A prediction gains credibility when it comes true. Before an event occurs, a prophecy may appear intriguing, inspiring, or even unlikely. After the event unfolds, those same words are often read in a new light. This simple observation lies at the heart of the argument being made here.

The claim is that the life of Jesus did not merely fulfill individual prophecies; it changed the way people viewed the Scriptures themselves. Before these events occurred, the ancient writings could be regarded as the religious literature of a particular people. But when readers began noticing connections between what had been written centuries earlier and what they believed had happened in their own time, they started asking a larger question: could these writings possess a wisdom that reaches beyond ordinary human foresight?

The argument does not rest only on specific predictions. It also points to the astonishing spread of the Christian message. A small group of followers, lacking wealth, political influence, and military power, carried their message across the known world. They traveled enormous distances, entered unfamiliar cultures, and spoke to people who had little reason to listen to them. Yet the movement continued to grow. This was not merely a historical curiosity. It suggested that a divine power was at work behind both the message and the messengers.

Still, there is another kind of evidence that interests us even more. Some truths are not proven by external events alone. They are recognized through experience. A person can be told that a piece of music is beautiful, but eventually they must listen for themselves. They can be told that a mountain view is breathtaking, but eventually they must stand before it. In the same way, there are dimensions of Scripture that become apparent only through careful reading and reflection.

This is why we must shift from history to personal encounter. The deepest testimony to the Scriptures is not merely that they contain fulfilled prophecies. It is that attentive readers often find themselves addressed by them in unexpected ways. A passage that once seemed ordinary suddenly opens into a deeper meaning. A story reveals wisdom that was previously hidden. A text written centuries ago speaks directly to present concerns. The reader begins to sense that there is more taking place than the exchange of information.

The coming of Christ, in this view, functions like the lifting of a veil. The ancient writings already contained profound truths, but many of those truths remained hidden until a new perspective made them visible. Just as sunlight streaming through a stained-glass window reveals colors that were always present but difficult to see, Christ illuminates meanings within Scripture that had previously been obscured. The text itself has not changed. What changes is the reader's ability to perceive what was there all along.

This leads to an important principle for approaching sacred writings. Not every truth lies on the surface. Some passages communicate their meaning immediately. Others require patience, contemplation, and maturity. The value of a text is not measured solely by how quickly it yields its treasures. Often the richest insights are found by those willing to linger. The invitation is not merely to read the words, but to enter into conversation with them until the deeper wisdom hidden beneath the letter begins to shine through.
