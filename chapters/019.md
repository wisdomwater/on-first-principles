## 19 — The Long Road Home

> *“They may return and be restored to their state of blessedness.”*

If freedom is real, then growth cannot be forced.

A soul may move toward God, or it may drift away. Some remain close to the good. Others wander far from it. This diversity, according to our reasoning, explains why creation contains such a wide variety of conditions and experiences. Not all rational creatures stand at the same place in their journey.

Yet none of these conditions should be mistaken for the final destination.

The present human life is not simply a test to determine who belongs and who does not. It is part of a larger work of healing. We find ourselves in a world of struggle because we are being taught, corrected, strengthened, and invited to return to the good. The difficulties of life are not necessarily signs of abandonment. They may be part of the medicine through which wounded souls are restored.

This also helps explain why existence feels so conflicted. We experience noble desires and selfish impulses, moments of clarity and moments of confusion. We encounter forces that encourage goodness and others that seem to pull us away from it. Human life is lived in the midst of a contest because we are not yet fully what we are meant to become.

Some, however, become so attached to selfishness and pride that they begin actively resisting the very healing that could restore them. These are the powers scripture describes as adversaries and opponents. Yet even here a question remains. Has evil become so deeply rooted that it can never be healed? Or does freedom, however wounded, always leave open the possibility of return?

On this point we should proceed carefully.

There are some who seem beyond recovery. Their opposition to the good appears complete. Yet if evil is not their original nature, if it arose through misuse of freedom, then one must at least ask whether the possibility of restoration can ever be entirely extinguished. Scripture does not provide a simple answer. It leaves us standing before a mystery.

What can be said is this: God's work of restoration is patient beyond anything we can imagine.

We measure change in days and years. God works through ages. Some souls may learn quickly. Others may require long correction and repeated instruction. Some may advance rapidly toward wisdom. Others may need countless opportunities to awaken. Divine providence does not abandon its task simply because the work is difficult.

Even the world itself participates in this process. Scripture speaks of a new heaven and a new earth. This suggests not the destruction of creation, but its renewal. Just as a person can be transformed without ceasing to be himself, so the world can be transformed without ceasing to be God's creation.

The final goal is not annihilation but harmony.

Everything that is false, broken, and distorted must pass away. Yet God's purpose is not merely to end things. It is to bring them to fulfillment. The scattered are gathered. The divided are reconciled. The wounded are healed. Creation itself is renewed.

What began in unity moves through freedom, struggle, and correction, and finally returns to unity once more.

The road may be longer than we imagine. It may pass through many stages beyond our present understanding. But the direction of the journey remains the same: from God, through all the complexities of freedom, and ultimately back toward God, who desires to be "all in all."
