## 12 — The Spirit and the Turning of the Soul

> *“The Spirit searcheth all things, even the deep things of God.”*

How do we come to know God?

At first glance, the answer might seem obvious. We learn through Scripture, through prayer, through reflection, and through the teachings of those who have gone before us. Yet if we look more carefully, another question emerges. What makes understanding possible in the first place? What awakens the mind to truths that were previously hidden?

We begin by emphasizing the unique role of the Holy Spirit. Just as the Son reveals the Father, the Spirit reveals the depths of God. This does not mean that the Spirit learns about God and then passes that knowledge along. Rather, the Spirit already knows. The Spirit belongs to the very life of God and therefore participates fully in the divine mystery. For this reason, the Spirit is not merely a messenger carrying information but a living presence that enables genuine understanding.

Salvation involves Father, Son, and Holy Spirit together. Why is the Spirit necessary? Why is it not enough simply to know that God exists or to understand something about Christ?

The activity of God unfolds in stages. The Father gives existence itself. Everything that exists participates in the Father's sustaining power. The Son, as the divine Word and Reason, gives intelligibility. Wherever there is rationality, understanding, or the capacity to recognize truth, the work of the Son is already present. Every human being shares in this gift simply by being capable of thought and moral awareness.

The work of the Holy Spirit, however, is different. The Spirit is not present merely because something exists or because someone possesses reason. The Spirit's activity appears when a person begins to turn toward what is good. When someone awakens to a deeper longing for truth, when the heart becomes receptive to transformation, when a life begins to move toward love, justice, humility, and wisdom, there the Spirit is at work.

Think of sunlight falling on a field. The light shines everywhere, but not every seed responds in the same way. Some remain dormant. Others begin to sprout and grow. The difference is not in the generosity of the light but in the condition of what receives it. In a similar way, the Father's sustaining presence and the Son's rational light are available to all. The Holy Spirit becomes active where the soul begins to respond, where growth and transformation have started.

This perspective helps explain why moral and spiritual growth are so closely connected. The Spirit is not simply concerned with what we know but with what we become. A person may possess intelligence and still remain trapped in selfishness. Knowledge alone does not heal the soul. The Spirit works at a deeper level, drawing the whole person toward participation in divine life.

Seen this way, the Christian journey is not merely an exercise in acquiring correct beliefs. It is a gradual turning of the entire self toward God. We live because the Father sustains us. We understand because the Son enlightens us. We are transformed because the Holy Spirit works within us. And as that transformation unfolds, the deeper things of God become not merely ideas we contemplate, but realities we begin to experience for ourselves.
