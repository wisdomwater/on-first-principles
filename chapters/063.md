## 63 — The Healer and the Willing Patient

> *"I will take away their stony heart and give them a heart of flesh."*

There is a question that naturally arises when we hear promises like this. If God is the one who removes a hardened heart and replaces it with a living one, then what role do we play? If transformation is God's work, does human effort matter at all? And if it does not, what becomes of freedom, responsibility, or the call to live a virtuous life?

At first glance, the language seems to suggest that everything depends entirely upon divine action. A hardened heart is softened. Ignorance is removed. New understanding is given. If God performs all of this, it might appear that human beings are merely passive recipients. Yet that conclusion misses something important. It overlooks the difference between receiving healing and being forced into it.

Imagine a person who recognizes that they lack knowledge and wisdom. After years of confusion, they decide to seek out a skilled teacher. They listen, ask questions, submit themselves to instruction, and commit themselves to learning. The teacher may rightly promise, "I will help you overcome your ignorance and grow in understanding." Yet no one would conclude that the student's choice was irrelevant. The teacher provides the knowledge, but the student must be willing to learn.

The same principle applies to spiritual growth. The promise of a new heart is not given to those who refuse all help, but to those who entrust themselves to the process of healing. A physician cannot heal a patient who will not come for treatment. A teacher cannot instruct a student who refuses to listen. The work of restoration belongs to the healer, yet the decision to seek healing belongs to the one who is sick.

This is why the examples of healing in the Gospels are so significant. Again and again, people come seeking help. The blind ask to see. The sick ask to be made well. Their coming, their trust, and their willingness matter. Yet the restoration itself remains the work of the healer. A blind person does not create sight through effort alone. The miracle lies beyond their power. What they can do is recognize their need and place themselves before the one who can help.

Seen in this light, divine grace and human freedom are not enemies. They work together. The soul does not transform itself by sheer determination, nor is it transformed against its will. Instead, growth begins when a person honestly recognizes their condition and willingly turns toward the source of healing. The more deeply they cooperate with that work, the more fully the promised transformation unfolds.

A hardened heart, then, is not overcome through self-improvement alone. Nor does it disappear without our participation. The change begins when we stop defending our condition, admit our need, and entrust ourselves to the One who can accomplish what we cannot. The new heart is a gift, but it is a gift received by those who open their hands to take it.
