## 10 — The Mirror of Goodness

> *“She is the brightness of the eternal light and an unspotted mirror of the working of God.”*

Suppose you stand before a perfectly clear mirror. Not a distorted mirror that stretches or shrinks what it reflects, but one so flawless that the image corresponds exactly to the reality before it. Whatever the original does, the reflection does. Whatever the original reveals, the reflection reveals. The mirror adds nothing of its own and takes nothing away.

This image lies at the heart of the passage before us. The Son is described as the brightness of God's eternal light and the spotless mirror of God's activity. These are not merely poetic titles. They are attempts to express something profound: that there is no gap between the Father's wisdom and the Son's wisdom, no contradiction between the Father's work and the Son's work, no division between the Father's goodness and the Son's goodness.

Think about how difficult it is for human beings to achieve such harmony. Even our best efforts are often mixed with misunderstanding, selfish motives, or incomplete knowledge. We imitate goodness, but imperfectly. We reflect truth, but partially. We participate in wisdom, but only in limited measure. The more closely we examine ourselves, the more we discover inconsistencies between what we believe and what we actually do.

The Son, however, is presented as the perfect revelation of the Father. Whatever God intends, the Son expresses. Whatever God accomplishes, the Son accomplishes. The relationship is not that of a student copying a teacher's work from a distance. It is more intimate than imitation. It is the complete correspondence of wisdom with wisdom, goodness with goodness, life with life.

This is why we return again to the image of eternal light. A lamp may be lit and later extinguished. A fire may burn brightly one moment and fade the next. But the light being described here is eternal. It has no beginning and no end. If the light is eternal, then its brightness cannot be something added later. The brightness belongs to the light itself. In the same way, the Son belongs eternally to the life of God.

We also can focus on goodness. We call many things good. We speak of good people, good decisions, good intentions, good books, and good deeds. Yet all these forms of goodness are fragile. They can increase or diminish. A kind person can become bitter. A wise person can make foolish choices. Our goodness is something we participate in rather than something we possess by nature.

The goodness of God is different. It does not fluctuate. It cannot be lost, exhausted, or corrupted. It is the source from which every other form of goodness flows. The Son is called the image of that goodness because everything found in the Father is expressed perfectly in him. Not a second goodness, not a lesser goodness, but the same goodness made visible and knowable.

This realization gathers together all the titles we have encountered so far. Wisdom, Word, Light, Truth, Life, Way, Power, and Goodness are not separate pieces of Christ's identity. They are different ways of describing the same reality. Through Christ, the hidden life of God becomes accessible. The invisible becomes knowable. The eternal shines into time. And through that light, we begin to see not only who Christ is, but who God has always been.
