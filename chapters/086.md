## 86 — Why Listen to Scripture?

> *"It seems necessary first of all to show that the scriptures themselves are divine."*

Before exploring what Scripture teaches, it is worth asking a more basic question: why should anyone trust it in the first place?

Every age has produced teachers, philosophers, reformers, and visionaries. Many have offered explanations of reality. Many have proposed systems of ethics, laws for society, or paths to wisdom. Most gained followers for a time. Some influenced a city, a nation, or a generation. Yet sooner or later their influence reached its natural limits. Their ideas remained largely confined to the people, language, or culture from which they emerged.

This raises an interesting question. What would we expect to see if a teaching truly carried a power beyond merely human persuasion? How would it differ from every other philosophy competing for attention in the marketplace of ideas?

Consider the challenge faced by any teacher. It is difficult enough to persuade a few people to change their habits. It is harder still to convince an entire community. To influence multiple nations, languages, and cultures is rarer still. Human beings are deeply attached to the customs they inherit. We usually cling to familiar beliefs, familiar stories, and familiar ways of life. Most movements struggle to break beyond those boundaries.

Yet there are moments in history when something unexpected happens. A message appears and begins crossing lines that normally divide people. It speaks to different cultures. It survives changes in language. It reaches people who have little in common with one another. It inspires individuals to leave behind long-established patterns of thought and embrace an entirely new vision of reality. When this occurs on a large scale, it becomes reasonable to ask whether more is happening than ordinary human influence.

This is the starting point of the argument. The spread of the biblical vision is not presented merely as evidence of good marketing, political power, or intellectual brilliance. The claim is that something within the message itself possesses a unique vitality. The writings associated with Moses shaped a people for centuries. The teachings associated with Christ crossed cultural boundaries that seemed impossible to cross. People from different nations, backgrounds, and traditions found themselves drawn toward the same source. The question is not simply whether the message spread, but why it spread in a way that so many other teachings did not.

Of course, widespread influence alone does not prove that something is true. Popularity is not the same thing as wisdom. But influence can be a clue worth examining. If a spring continues to nourish people generation after generation, it is reasonable to wonder what kind of water is flowing from it. The enduring power of a teaching invites investigation rather than dismissal.

This is why the discussion now turns toward Scripture itself. Before considering its individual teachings, it is necessary to ask what kind of book could exercise such a lasting influence. The argument begins not with abstract theories about inspiration but with a simple observation: many voices have spoken throughout history, yet only a few continue to speak across centuries, cultures, and languages as though they possess a life of their own. The task is to discover whether Scripture belongs among those rare voices.
