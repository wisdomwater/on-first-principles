## 49 — What Do We Really Desire?

> *"The highest good is the pleasure of the body, another the service of the State, and another devotion to wisdom and knowledge."*

Every person lives for something.

One person seeks pleasure. Another pursues success, influence, or achievement. Another devotes himself to learning, discovery, or the search for truth. Whatever we regard as the highest good eventually becomes the center around which our life revolves. Our time, energy, attention, and desires gradually organize themselves around what we love most.

This raises an important question: If there is a life beyond this one, what kind of life should we expect it to be?

Many people naturally imagine heaven as an improved version of earth. If they enjoy food, they picture an endless banquet. If they enjoy wealth, they imagine unimaginable riches. If they value power, they picture themselves ruling kingdoms. The future becomes an extension of present desires, only larger and more secure. In this way of thinking, the promises of Scripture are interpreted almost entirely in material terms. Golden cities are expected to be literal cities. Feasts are expected to be literal feasts. Prosperity is expected to be literal prosperity.

Yet there is a problem with this picture. If the highest blessing consists merely in obtaining more of the things we already possess, then the future life differs from the present one only in degree. It becomes a continuation of earthly existence rather than a transformation of it.

To see why this matters, consider how desires mature. A child may think happiness consists in having unlimited sweets. An adult understands that health, friendship, wisdom, and love are far greater goods. The child is not wrong to desire something pleasant, but his vision is incomplete. In much the same way, spiritual growth gradually teaches us that there are joys greater than bodily satisfaction. The deepest hunger of the soul is not for food but for truth. The deepest thirst is not for drink but for understanding, beauty, goodness, and communion with God.

This is why the promises of Scripture are often expressed through symbols. Bread becomes a symbol of divine wisdom that nourishes the soul. Wine becomes a symbol of joy and participation in the life of God. A kingdom becomes a symbol of spiritual maturity and participation in divine purposes. The language is drawn from earthly experience because earthly things provide the nearest images we possess. Yet the realities themselves surpass the images used to describe them.

The question beneath all of this is surprisingly personal. What do you actually desire? If the things of God were offered to you, would you recognize them as treasures? Or would you still prefer the familiar satisfactions of the present world? The answer reveals much about the condition of the soul. We tend to imagine heaven according to what we love now.

The promises, then, are not merely rewards waiting in the future. They are invitations to cultivate new desires in the present. The more a person grows in wisdom, truth, and love, the more he begins to understand what the promises are truly pointing toward. The greatest gift God offers is not endless consumption but endless participation in the life of the Good itself. And compared with that, even the finest pleasures of earth are only shadows of a greater feast.
