## 75 — The Strength to Endure

> *"God will give a way of escape, that ye may be able to bear it."*

When we face a difficult trial, we often wish for certainty. We want assurance that we will succeed, that we will remain faithful, that we will emerge victorious in the end. Yet notice the difference between being given victory and being given the ability to fight.

The promise is not that every struggle will be easy, nor even that we will always overcome. The promise is that we will never be abandoned without resources. Strength is provided. Help is available. A path remains open. What happens next depends in part upon how we respond to what has been given.

This distinction matters. If success were guaranteed, there would be no real struggle. Courage would lose its meaning because there would be no possibility of fear. Perseverance would lose its value because failure would be impossible. The nobility of the contest lies precisely in the fact that the outcome is not predetermined. We are given the ability to stand, but we must still choose to stand.

Yet no one fights alone. Human strength by itself is limited. Even the wisest, strongest, and most faithful people encounter situations beyond their own resources. Again and again, growth requires a strength that does not originate within us. The greatest saints are not those who rely most heavily on themselves, but those who learn how deeply they depend upon divine help.

This is why spiritual struggle is often misunderstood. We imagine the battle is against circumstances themselves—loss, disappointment, criticism, illness, uncertainty, or grief. But these things are often only the battlefield, not the enemy. The deeper struggle concerns what these experiences awaken within us. Will suffering lead to bitterness? Will disappointment produce despair? Will injustice tempt us toward hatred? Will hardship persuade us that God has abandoned us?

Consider the story of Job. The true contest was never about livestock, wealth, health, or even personal tragedy. The question beneath every loss was whether Job would cease to trust the goodness of God. The outward events were severe, but the real battle was taking place within the heart.

Seen in this light, many of life's trials take on a different meaning. Difficult circumstances are not automatically signs of divine displeasure, nor are they proof that we have been forsaken. They become occasions in which character, faith, and understanding are tested and strengthened. The question is not whether hardship will come. It will. The question is what that hardship will shape within us.

The gift of God is not a life free from struggle. It is the presence, strength, and grace that make endurance possible. We are not promised that every battle will be won effortlessly. We are promised that no battle need be faced alone.
