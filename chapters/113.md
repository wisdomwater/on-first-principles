## 113 — The Light and Its Radiance

> *“As light can never exist without its brightness, so neither can the Son be conceived as existing without the Father.”*

When we speak about God, we inevitably run into the limits of language. We borrow words from ordinary life—father, son, wisdom, word, light—not because they perfectly describe divine reality, but because they help us glimpse something that would otherwise remain beyond our reach.

One question that troubled many people was whether the Son came into existence at some point, as though God first existed alone and only later produced the Son. But consider the image of light and its radiance. Can there be a light that shines no brightness? The moment light exists, its radiance exists with it. You cannot separate the two in time. One does not wait for the other to appear.

The same reasoning applies to God's wisdom, truth, and life. If there had ever been a moment when the Son did not exist, there would have been a moment when God was without wisdom, without truth, without the living Word through whom all things are known. Yet that is impossible. God is not something that gradually acquires wisdom or becomes truthful over time. These qualities belong to God's very being. Therefore the divine Word, who is called Wisdom and Truth, cannot be understood as something added later.

Even here we must be careful. The phrase "there was never a time" is useful, but it can also mislead us. We naturally think in terms of before and after, earlier and later. God does not. Time itself belongs to the created order. Before there were ages, seasons, histories, or worlds, God already was. The relationship between the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit exists beyond the flow of time altogether. We are trying to describe an eternal reality using words designed for clocks and calendars.

This also means that God cannot be confined to a location. We often think of presence in physical terms. If someone is here, they are not there. But wisdom does not occupy space the way a chair does. Truth is not locked inside a room. In the same way, the divine Word is present wherever its power and life are active. Christ was present in Paul, present in Peter, present in the saints, and present among the heavenly beings. Not as a body divided into pieces, but as a living reality that can be shared without being diminished.

Think of sunlight streaming through many windows. The light enters each room, yet it remains one light. Some rooms may receive it more fully because their windows are larger or cleaner, but the source remains unchanged. So it is with the divine presence. Different beings participate in God's life to different degrees, yet God remains whole and undivided.

The point of all this is not to solve the mystery of God but to guard us from imagining God as merely a larger version of ourselves. God is not a body occupying a place, nor a being who changes from ignorance to wisdom or from solitude to relationship. The Father, Son, and Holy Spirit belong to a reality deeper than time, larger than space, and more fundamental than anything we can see. The images of light, wisdom, and word are invitations to lift our thinking beyond the visible world and glimpse something of that eternal life from which all things come.
