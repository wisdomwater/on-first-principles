## 2 — The Courage to Leave Some Questions Open

> *"Some things were delivered plainly, while concerning others the apostles left the reason and manner to be investigated."*

One of the most striking things about Origen is how comfortable he is with mystery.

Many people imagine that the great theologians of the early Church believed they had answers for everything. Yet in this passage Origen repeatedly points out how much remains uncertain. The Church, he says, has received certain truths clearly: God exists, Christ came among us, the Holy Spirit is at work, human beings possess freedom, and life has meaning beyond death. These are the foundations.

But beyond those foundations lie many questions. What exactly is the soul? How does it come into being? What are angels? What is the precise nature of spiritual beings? What existed before the world began? What will exist after its present form passes away? On these matters, Origen notes, the apostles often left room for inquiry rather than providing detailed explanations.

This might seem unsettling at first. We often long for certainty. We want every mystery resolved, every question answered, every loose thread tied neatly together. Yet Origen sees something valuable in unanswered questions. They invite us to grow. If every truth could be reduced to a simple formula, wisdom would require little effort. But some realities are so deep that they can only be approached through reflection, study, prayer, and experience.

Think of the difference between reading about friendship and actually having a lifelong friend. One can be described in a few sentences. The other unfolds over years and continually reveals new depths. Origen believed many spiritual truths are like that. They are not puzzles waiting to be solved so much as realities waiting to be explored.

This attitude also shapes the way he approaches Scripture. Origen argues that the sacred writings contain more than their surface meaning. Just as a seed contains more than what is visible from the outside, Scripture contains layers of insight that unfold as a person matures in understanding. The obvious meaning is important, but it is not always the end of the journey. Beneath familiar words may lie deeper wisdom waiting to be discovered.

Perhaps the most refreshing aspect of this passage is Origen's confidence that faith and inquiry belong together. He does not present questions as threats to belief. Nor does he suggest that curiosity is a sign of weakness. Instead, he sees thoughtful investigation as part of the spiritual life itself. The search for understanding is not a departure from faith; it is one of faith's natural expressions.

In our own age, people often feel pressured into one of two camps. Some insist that every question must have a definite answer. Others conclude that because not everything can be known, nothing can be known. Origen chooses a different path. Hold firmly to what is clear, he says, but remain humble before what is not. There is wisdom in conviction, but there is also wisdom in wonder.

Perhaps maturity is not measured by how many mysteries we eliminate, but by how faithfully we learn to live with them.

**Thought to Ponder**

*What unanswered question in your life might be less a problem to solve and more an invitation to grow in wisdom?*
