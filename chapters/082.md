## 82 — The Healing Meaning of Subjection

> *"When all things have been subjected unto him, then shall the Son also himself be subjected unto him that did subject all things unto him."*

Some words carry a negative weight for us. "Subjection" is one of them. We hear it and immediately think of domination, coercion, or the loss of freedom. Yet there is another way to understand it. What if subjection, in its deepest sense, is not about being crushed beneath another's power but about being brought into harmony with what is good?

Consider the passage that speaks of the Son being subjected to the Father at the end of all things. Some have assumed this means the Son somehow becomes less than he was before. But that interpretation creates more problems than it solves. How could the one who reigns over all creation, who brings everything into order, somehow become diminished at the very moment his work reaches completion? The image makes little sense. A master builder is not reduced when the building is finished. The completion of the work reveals the success of the work.

The point is not that the Son loses anything. Rather, the Son presents the completed work of restoration to the Father. Everything that was broken has been healed. Everything that was scattered has been gathered. Everything that resisted goodness has been brought back into right relationship. The Son's "subjection" is the joyful completion of a mission freely embraced from the beginning. It is the offering of a restored creation back to its source.

This understanding changes the meaning of another difficult phrase: the subjection of Christ's enemies. If Christ's own subjection is something good, then the subjection of his enemies cannot simply mean their destruction. Instead, it points toward their healing. An enemy is someone out of alignment with truth, goodness, and love. To become subject to Christ is to be restored to those realities. The goal is not annihilation but transformation. The language may sound severe, but the movement is medicinal rather than punitive.

How does such a restoration happen? Not through force. Not through irresistible power. Not through the erasure of freedom. People are changed through understanding, experience, instruction, correction, and growth. Think of the difference between a small child and a mature adult. A child may need warnings and consequences because they cannot yet understand the reasons behind a rule. A mature person acts differently. They see the good for themselves and willingly choose it. Fear gives way to understanding. Compulsion gives way to freedom.

This does not mean every person follows the same path or learns at the same pace. Some are ready for deeper truths. Others need more time. Some respond quickly to wisdom. Others resist until hardship teaches what gentler lessons could not. The workings of grace are infinitely varied because people are infinitely varied. Yet beneath all these different paths lies a common purpose: not to overpower the will but to heal it. The wisdom of God does not seek slaves. It seeks free beings who have come to recognize the good and embrace it as their own. When that work is complete, subjection and freedom will no longer appear as opposites. They will be revealed as two names for the same reality: a will joyfully united with the source of all life.
