## 93 — Learning How to Read

> *"It is dangerous to declare lightly that we understand things for which the key of knowledge is necessary."*

Once we recognize that Scripture contains depths beyond its surface meaning, a new challenge appears. How do we uncover those depths? It is one thing to say that a passage contains hidden wisdom. It is another thing entirely to understand what that wisdom is.

Many people sense that the stories of Scripture point beyond themselves. They read about strange family histories, elaborate rituals, prophetic visions, or symbolic objects and instinctively feel that something more is being communicated. Yet recognizing a mystery and interpreting a mystery are not the same thing. The first is relatively easy. The second requires patience, humility, and wisdom.

Think about how often this happens in ordinary life. You may hear a piece of music and know immediately that it carries profound emotion. Yet explaining exactly why it moves you can be difficult. You know there is meaning present, but putting that meaning into words requires careful listening and mature understanding. Sacred writings often work in a similar way. They reveal enough to awaken wonder, but not always enough to satisfy a casual glance.

This is why spiritual understanding cannot be rushed. Some people fail because they do not give the task sufficient effort. Others fail because they assume they already know the answer before they begin the search. Both mistakes close the door to deeper insight. Wisdom grows in the person who is willing to remain a student, who approaches difficult passages with reverence rather than certainty.

The same principle applies throughout Scripture. The prophets speak in riddles and images. The Gospels contain layers of meaning that continue to unfold long after the stories are first read. Even the letters of the apostles, which often appear straightforward, contain depths that generations of readers have continued to explore. Again and again, the text hints at realities that extend beyond what can be grasped at first glance. It is as though shafts of light shine through narrow openings, allowing us to glimpse vast rooms beyond.

For this reason, confidence should be balanced with humility. It is easy to mistake familiarity for understanding. We may know the words, recognize the stories, and repeat the teachings, yet still miss what lies beneath them. The wisest readers are often those most aware of how much remains to be discovered.

The goal is not to solve every mystery but to learn how to approach mystery well. Sacred writings are not puzzles to be conquered so much as landscapes to be explored. The deeper we travel, the more we realize that understanding is not merely the result of intelligence. It is also the fruit of patience, openness, and a willingness to let truth reveal itself gradually. The key to knowledge is not possession of all the answers. It is learning how to seek.
