## 34 — A Good Tree

> *“The law is holy, and the commandment holy and just and good.”*

Sometimes disagreements begin not because people see different facts, but because they use words differently.

Much of the debate surrounding God's nature comes down to a simple question: What do we mean when we call something good?

Many people use the word narrowly. They reserve it for kindness, generosity, gentleness, and mercy. Justice, wisdom, courage, discipline, and truthfulness are placed in separate categories, as though they were different virtues competing for influence. Under this way of thinking, a person might be good without being just, or just without being good.

But that is not how virtue works.

Imagine describing someone as a good person. What do you mean by that? Surely you mean more than that they are merely pleasant. You mean they are honest. Fair. Trustworthy. Compassionate. Wise. Courageous when courage is needed. Patient when patience is required. The word *good* gathers many virtues together under a single name.

This is why it makes little sense to separate goodness and justice as though they belonged to different worlds. Justice is not a rival to goodness. It is one of the forms goodness takes. Just as a healthy tree produces many branches while remaining one tree, the various virtues grow from the same root. We may distinguish them for the sake of discussion, but they are not truly independent of one another.

The same principle applies when we speak about God. If goodness is the larger reality, then justice belongs within it rather than outside it. A just act may not always feel pleasant, just as pruning a tree may look destructive for a season. Yet pruning serves the life of the tree. In the same way, justice serves the purposes of goodness rather than opposing them.

This insight also helps us read Scripture more carefully. We often notice passages that support our preferred picture of God while overlooking those that do not. Some readers focus on texts that speak of mercy and conclude that God is only gentle. Others focus on judgment and conclude that God is only severe. Yet the Scriptures themselves resist these simplifications. The same God is called good. The same God is called just. The same God is described as merciful, holy, patient, and righteous.

The challenge, then, is not deciding which of these qualities truly belongs to God. The challenge is learning to hold them together. Reality is usually richer than our categories allow.

When Jesus speaks of a good tree producing good fruit, the image points beyond a single virtue. Healthy roots give rise to many expressions of life. In the same way, divine goodness is not merely one attribute among others. It is the living source from which justice, mercy, wisdom, holiness, and love all flow. The more deeply we understand goodness, the more we discover that it contains far more than kindness alone.

And the more we understand God, the less need we feel to divide what was never meant to be separated.
