## 98 — Hearing the Law

> *"Ye that desire to be under the law, do ye not hear the law?"*

There is a difference between reading words and truly hearing them. We all know this from experience. A person can listen to a conversation while thinking about something else entirely. The sounds reach their ears, but the meaning never reaches their understanding. The same thing can happen when reading sacred texts.

This is the concern being raised when Scripture asks, "Do you not hear the law?" The question is not whether the words have been read. The words may be perfectly familiar. The question is whether their deeper meaning has been grasped. Have we understood what the text is trying to communicate, or have we stopped at the surface?

Many people assume that understanding a passage means knowing the story. They can recount the events, identify the characters, and remember the sequence. Yet Scripture often invites a deeper kind of hearing. It asks us to consider why the story was preserved, what truth it reveals, and how it speaks beyond its immediate setting.

Consider a parable told by a wise teacher. One listener remembers only the plot. Another understands the lesson hidden within it. Both heard the same words, but only one truly heard the meaning. The difference is not intelligence. It is attention. One listened for information; the other listened for insight.

This is why the apostles often read familiar passages in ways that surprised their audiences. They discovered patterns, symbols, and connections that pointed beyond the immediate narrative. When they spoke of allegory or deeper meaning, they were not dismissing the original story. They were showing that the story contained more than many readers had noticed.

Such reading requires humility. If we assume that the first thing we see is all there is to see, we close ourselves to further understanding. Yet if we approach the text with curiosity and patience, we begin to notice layers that were previously hidden. The same words that once seemed simple begin to reveal unexpected richness.

This does not mean every verse conceals a secret code or that every detail demands elaborate interpretation. Rather, it means that Scripture rewards attentive reading. It asks us to engage not only our memory but our imagination, not only our intellect but our spiritual perception.

To hear the law, then, is more than reading the text. It is allowing the text to speak. It is listening long enough for the deeper meaning to emerge. When that happens, familiar passages often become new again. What once appeared ordinary begins to shine with significance, and we discover that the words were carrying more wisdom than we first imagined.
