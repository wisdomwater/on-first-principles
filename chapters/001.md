## 1 — Seeking Wisdom at the Source

> *"We must learn the truth from him."*

Origen begins his great work with a simple but profound conviction: if Christ is the Truth, then every sincere search for truth must ultimately lead back to him.

At first glance, this may sound obvious for a Christian writer. Yet Origen is addressing a problem that feels surprisingly modern. Even in the early centuries of Christianity, believers disagreed with one another. Different teachers offered different interpretations. Questions arose about God, Christ, the Holy Spirit, and the meaning of Scripture. Some disagreements were minor. Others touched the very heart of the faith.

Faced with this confusion, Origen does not suggest abandoning the search. Nor does he suggest accepting every claim that presents itself as spiritual wisdom. Instead, he encourages discernment. Just as a traveler must learn which guides know the road and which merely claim to know it, those seeking spiritual truth must learn how to distinguish reliable wisdom from mistaken certainty.

One of Origen's most beautiful insights appears near the beginning of this passage. He argues that Christ did not suddenly appear in history without preparation. The same divine wisdom that spoke through Jesus was already at work through Moses, the prophets, and all who genuinely sought God. In other words, God's voice did not begin in one moment and remain silent everywhere else. The story of divine truth stretches through generations, patiently guiding humanity toward greater understanding.

Yet Origen is equally convinced that not every question has an easy answer. The apostles, he says, taught certain truths plainly because everyone needed them. But they also left some matters unexplained. This was not a failure on their part. It was an invitation. Some truths must be explored rather than merely received. Spiritual maturity involves learning how to think deeply, wrestle honestly, and grow in wisdom over time.

There is something refreshing about this perspective. We often assume that faith and questioning stand on opposite sides of a divide. Origen saw them differently. For him, faith was not the end of inquiry but its beginning. A person could trust God deeply while continuing to explore difficult questions. Indeed, the desire to understand more fully was itself a sign of spiritual life.

Perhaps that is why these opening pages still speak to us today. We live in a world overflowing with opinions, experts, influencers, and competing claims about what is true. The challenge is not merely finding information. The challenge is learning where wisdom lives. Origen's answer is that genuine wisdom begins with humility, remains anchored in what is most essential, and never loses its desire to seek deeper understanding. Truth is not merely something we possess. It is something we continually learn to love.

**Thought to Ponder**

*When you encounter uncertainty or disagreement, do you tend to cling more tightly to your existing beliefs, or do you allow the question to become an invitation to deeper wisdom?*
