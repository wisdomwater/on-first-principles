## 1 — Where Do We Begin?

> *“Grace and truth came through Jesus Christ.”*

If you wanted to learn how to live well, where would you begin? There are countless voices offering answers. Some promise wisdom through philosophy. Others through self-improvement, success, wealth, or power. Yet before we can make progress, we must first decide whom we trust to teach us.

The Christian faith begins with the conviction that truth is not merely an idea but something revealed through Christ. This does not mean that truth suddenly appeared when Jesus was born in Bethlehem. Rather, the same divine wisdom that spoke through Jesus was already at work long before. It was present in the prophets, guiding them. It was present in Moses, shaping his understanding. Whenever people encountered genuine light, justice, mercy, or wisdom, they were encountering the activity of the same divine Word that would later become visible in Christ.

This raises an important question. If Christ is the source of truth, why do people who claim to follow him so often disagree? Even in the earliest centuries of the Church, believers held different opinions about God, Christ, the Holy Spirit, and many other important matters. The existence of disagreement does not remove the need to seek truth. If anything, it makes the search more necessary. When travelers encounter many roads, they must become more attentive, not less.

Imagine arriving in a city you've never visited before. Ten people offer directions. Some point east, others west. Some speak confidently, yet their instructions contradict one another. At some point you must decide which guide is trustworthy. The challenge is not merely finding a voice but finding a voice rooted in reality. In matters of faith, the task is similar. We must learn how to distinguish passing opinions from what has been faithfully handed down and carefully preserved.

Yet not every question receives an immediate answer. The earliest teachers of the faith proclaimed certain truths plainly because everyone needed them. They taught that God is the creator of all things. They taught that Christ came from the Father and entered human life for our salvation. They taught the foundations openly. But they did not explain every mystery in detail. Some questions were left for later generations to ponder, explore, and wrestle with. The life of faith is not merely about receiving answers; it is also about growing into the kind of person capable of asking deeper questions.

This is where the journey begins. Before exploring the mysteries of God, we must first decide where we will seek wisdom and whom we will trust as our guide. Once that foundation is laid, the deeper questions become not threats to faith but invitations into it. The search for understanding is not a departure from spiritual life. It is one of the ways spiritual life matures.
