## 1 — Seeking Wisdom at the Source

> *"We must learn the truth from him."*

Where do we go when we want to know what is true?

It is a simple question, but one that every generation must answer for itself. The world is full of voices competing for our attention. Some promise certainty. Others promise freedom. Some offer wisdom, while others merely offer confidence. The challenge is not finding opinions. The challenge is learning where wisdom lives.

The Christian faith begins with the conviction that truth is not merely a collection of ideas. Truth is something living. Truth has a face. Truth is revealed through Christ. This does not mean that every question receives an immediate answer, nor does it mean that every mystery disappears. It means that the search itself has a center.

Long before Christ walked the roads of Galilee, people were already listening for the voice of God. The prophets listened. Moses listened. The righteous sought understanding. The same divine wisdom that would later be revealed in Jesus was already at work, drawing humanity toward what is good, true, and beautiful. The story of God is larger than a single moment. It is a long conversation unfolding through history.

Yet sincere people often disagree. They did in the ancient world, and they do today. Questions about God, the soul, and the meaning of life have always generated differing opinions. Faced with this reality, we can become cynical, convinced that truth is impossible to find. Or we can become rigid, refusing to question anything at all.

There is another path. Hold fast to what is most essential while remaining willing to seek deeper understanding. Wisdom grows when conviction and humility walk together. We need roots, but we also need curiosity.

Some truths are clear enough to guide a life. Others require patience, reflection, and years of growth. The search for understanding is not a sign of weak faith. It is often a sign of living faith. A heart that loves truth will continue seeking it.

The journey begins by recognizing that wisdom is not something we manufacture for ourselves. It is something we learn to receive.
