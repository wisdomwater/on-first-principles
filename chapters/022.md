## 22 — Difference Without Favoritism

> *"The cause of the diversity and variety among these beings is shown to be derived not from any unfairness on the part of the Disposer but from their own actions."*

One of the oldest questions people ask is why some beings seem to be given greater responsibilities than others. Why does one person become a teacher while another becomes a student? Why does one seem naturally suited for leadership while another serves quietly behind the scenes? Extending the question further, why would one angel be entrusted with great responsibilities while another is given a more modest role?

At first glance, the easiest answer is that some creatures were simply made greater than others. Perhaps some souls were created noble, while others were created ordinary. Yet that explanation creates a troubling problem. If God assigns higher and lower positions without regard to anything the creature has done, then the differences we see would reflect favoritism rather than justice. The order of creation would begin to look arbitrary, as though some were simply lucky enough to be born into privilege while others were not.

The alternative presented here is that differences of responsibility arise from differences in character, faithfulness, and willingness. Just as a wise employer does not assign the same task to every worker regardless of ability, God entrusts responsibilities according to what each soul has become. The various ranks of angels are not signs that God loves some more than others. Rather, they reflect the different ways rational creatures have responded to the opportunities placed before them. What appears to be hierarchy is, in this view, the result of growth and development rather than favoritism.

To support this idea, consider two of the most revered figures in the Christian story: Peter and Paul. Both eventually became pillars of the faith. Yet both also stumbled badly. Peter denied even knowing Jesus. Paul persecuted the very movement he later devoted his life to advancing. Their failures matter because they reveal something important about human nature. Neither man was locked into goodness from the beginning. Neither was incapable of error. Their greatness emerged not because they belonged to a special class of souls, but because they changed, learned, and grew.

This leads to a larger principle. Every rational creature possesses the capacity for both good and evil. That capacity does not mean everyone will become wicked, nor does it mean everyone will become virtuous. It simply means that freedom is real. A person may become wise or foolish, compassionate or cruel, courageous or fearful. The possibility of either path is built into what it means to be a rational being.

This understanding preserves both justice and hope. Justice, because no one's position is merely assigned by accident or favoritism. Hope, because no one is permanently trapped by what they have been. The saint once struggled. The sinner may yet become holy. Growth remains possible because freedom remains alive. The story of a soul is never determined solely by its beginning. What matters is the direction in which it chooses to move.
