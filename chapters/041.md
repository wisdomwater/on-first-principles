## 41 — The Fire That Grows Cold

> *"Turn unto thy rest, O my soul."*

Have you ever noticed that there are times when you feel fully alive, clear-minded, and deeply connected to what matters, and other times when that sense of life seems distant? The question explored here is whether that distance is part of our true nature or whether it is the result of having drifted from something greater.

Consider the difference between a glowing ember and one that has nearly gone dark. Both are still embers, yet one radiates warmth while the other barely retains any heat. In much the same way, there seems to be a distinction between the highest part of ourselves—the part capable of perceiving truth, goodness, and God—and the ordinary condition in which most of us live. We may sense moments of insight, beauty, or spiritual clarity, yet we often find ourselves distracted, divided, and forgetful of what we know is most important.

This idea is illustrated throughout Scripture by the image of fire. God is described as fire. The angels are associated with flame. The saints are urged to be fervent in spirit. Fire represents life, energy, love, and participation in the divine. By contrast, spiritual decline is often described in terms of coldness. Love grows cold. The powers opposed to God are linked with icy waters, darkness, and the chill of separation. The imagery suggests that our deepest problem is not that we were created defective, but that we have lost some of the warmth for which we were made.

Seen in this light, the soul can be understood as a being in the process of restoration. We often think of salvation as rescuing something from danger, but rescue is only part of the story. When a lost traveler finds the road home, he does not merely stop being lost. He becomes once again what he was meant to be. In the same way, the soul's journey is not simply escape from wrongdoing or suffering. It is a return to its proper condition. The goal is not merely survival, but transformation.

This perspective also helps explain why people seem so different from one another. Some appear naturally receptive to truth, wisdom, and compassion, while others struggle to perceive them at all. Rather than seeing every person as equally awake or equally asleep, we might imagine humanity as existing at many different distances from the fire. Some retain much of its warmth. Others only a faint spark. Yet wherever there is even the smallest ember, the possibility of renewed flame remains.

The hopeful note running beneath the entire discussion is that coldness is not final. A fire that has dimmed may burn brightly again. The soul may wander, forget, and lose its way, yet it retains the capacity to return. That is why the ancient invitation remains so powerful: "Return unto thy rest." The deepest movement of the spiritual life is not becoming something entirely new. It is becoming once more what, in some profound sense, we have always been meant to be.
