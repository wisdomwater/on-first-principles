## 107 — The Inner Israel

> *“They are not all Israel, who are of Israel.”*

Once we begin to see that scripture often speaks on more than one level, an important question emerges: Who are the people described in its pages? Are the names Israel, Judah, and Jerusalem referring only to nations, tribes, and cities of the ancient world, or might they also point toward deeper realities?

The apostle Paul repeatedly encourages his readers to look beneath the surface. He speaks of an Israel that is more than a physical nation. He speaks of a circumcision that is more than a physical mark. He even says that not everyone who belongs to Israel outwardly belongs to the deeper reality symbolized by Israel. In other words, the visible people and places of scripture may serve as images of spiritual realities that transcend geography and ancestry.

Think of how we use language today. We may speak of someone's "homeland" without referring merely to a country on a map. We may describe a person as being "at home" when they have found peace, purpose, or belonging. Physical places often become symbols of inward realities. Scripture frequently works in the same way. The historical nation remains real, but it may also point toward a larger community defined not by bloodline but by its relationship to God.

This becomes especially important when we read the promises of the prophets. Many passages speak of astonishing blessings prepared for Israel, Jerusalem, and the people of God. Yet if we read these promises only in a narrowly earthly sense, they often seem strangely limited compared to the grandeur of the promises themselves. The language reaches beyond politics, territory, and national prosperity. It points toward a deeper fulfillment.

What if Israel represents all those who seek God? What if Jerusalem is more than a city of stone and streets? The New Testament itself begins moving in this direction when it speaks of a heavenly Jerusalem and calls it our mother. Suddenly the geography of scripture expands. The reader discovers that the ancient names are not being discarded but enlarged. They become windows into realities that encompass far more than a single people or place.

This way of reading transforms the relationship between the reader and the text. The stories are no longer only about someone else's ancestors, someone else's city, or someone else's covenant. The drama becomes personal. The journey of Israel becomes the journey of every soul learning to see God. The struggles, exiles, restorations, and homecomings described throughout scripture begin to mirror movements taking place within the human heart.

The deeper message, then, is not that the historical Israel was unreal. It is that the visible Israel points beyond itself. Just as a shadow reveals the shape of something greater, the outward story directs our attention toward a spiritual reality that includes all who are being drawn into the life of God. The names remain the same, but their horizon becomes immeasurably larger.
