## 100 — The Great Questions

> *"The Holy Spirit was pre-eminently concerned with the unspeakable mysteries connected with the affairs of men."*

At the heart of Scripture lies a remarkable ambition. Its purpose is not merely to preserve history, establish laws, or record religious practices. It seeks to illuminate the deepest questions a human being can ask.

Who is God?

What is the nature of reality?

Why do human beings experience both greatness and brokenness?

What is the purpose of life?

Why is there suffering?

How can we be restored?

According to this vision, the prophets and apostles were not primarily concerned with satisfying curiosity about the past. Their aim was to guide people toward wisdom about the most important realities of existence. Historical events, laws, stories, and symbols all serve this larger purpose.

This helps explain why Scripture often moves so quickly from visible events to invisible truths. A journey through a desert becomes a lesson about trust. A kingdom rises and falls, revealing something about the human heart. A sacrifice points toward reconciliation. A promise points toward hope. The external event matters, but its deepest significance lies in what it reveals about the human condition and our relationship with God.

Imagine reading a great novel. The plot is important, but few readers would say the book is merely about the events that occur. What matters most are the questions beneath the story: love, loss, courage, identity, forgiveness, and meaning. The narrative becomes a vehicle for exploring realities that transcend the specific characters involved. Scripture often operates in a similar way, though on an even larger scale.

The central focus, however, remains the knowledge of God. Everything else ultimately circles back to this point. Questions about humanity, suffering, destiny, and salvation derive their significance from the larger question of who God is and how creation relates to its source. To understand ourselves fully, we must understand something of the One from whom we come.

This is why the biblical writers continually direct attention beyond the immediate circumstances of life. They are concerned not only with what happens but with why it happens. Not only with human actions but with divine purposes. Not only with the visible world but with the realities that sustain it.

When read in this way, Scripture becomes a guide to the great questions rather than a collection of isolated answers. It invites us into a lifelong exploration of God, humanity, and creation. The stories, symbols, and teachings are not ends in themselves. They are signposts pointing toward truths large enough to occupy a soul for an entire lifetime.
