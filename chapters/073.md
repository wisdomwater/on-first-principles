## 73 — The Door We Leave Open

> *"The flesh lusteth against the spirit, and the spirit against the flesh."*

It is tempting to blame every moral failure on some force outside ourselves. If only the right circumstances existed, if only the right influences surrounded us, if only temptation disappeared, then surely we would live wisely and well.

But consider something as simple as hunger. Hunger is not evil. Thirst is not evil. The desire for companionship, pleasure, rest, achievement, or intimacy is not evil. These impulses belong to our nature. They are part of what it means to be human.

The problem begins when a good thing escapes its proper boundaries. Hunger becomes gluttony. The desire for security becomes greed. Natural attraction becomes obsession. Anger at injustice becomes rage. Grief becomes despair. The impulse itself is not the enemy. The danger lies in allowing it to grow beyond the measure for which it was given.

This is why not every temptation can be blamed on unseen powers. Even without any external influence, human beings would still struggle with excess, impatience, carelessness, and poor judgment. We do not need someone to teach us every mistake we make. Often the seeds are already present within us.

Yet this does not mean that destructive influences play no role. Once a small compromise is accepted, it tends to grow. A little greed seeks more. A little resentment deepens into bitterness. A little dishonesty becomes a habit. What begins as a moment of weakness can gradually expand until it takes on a life of its own. It is as though every unchecked impulse opens a door through which larger forces can enter and magnify what was already there.

Think of a tiny crack in a dam. The crack itself may seem insignificant, but once water begins flowing through it, the pressure widens the opening. What began as a small weakness becomes a growing breach. In much the same way, our first acts of intemperance create opportunities for destructive patterns to gain strength.

Yet this is not a message of fear. It is a message of responsibility and hope. We are not helpless victims of either our desires or the forces that seek to exploit them. The struggle is real, but so is our capacity to meet it. The purpose of discipline, self-awareness, and virtue is not to suppress our humanity but to keep our natural desires within their proper bounds, where they can serve life rather than undermine it.

The good news is that every temptation contains a possibility of endurance. We may not always feel strong enough, and we may not always choose wisely, but the capacity to resist is never entirely absent. Growth begins when we learn to recognize the small openings through which excess enters and gently close them before they become wide enough to reshape our lives.
