## 61 — Learning Our Need

> *"Why, Lord, didst thou harden our heart, that we should not fear thy name?"*

At first glance, it can seem strange to blame God for a hardened heart. How could the One who desires our good be responsible for our wandering? Yet there is another way to understand the question. What if the complaint is not that God actively caused the hardness, but that he allowed it to continue? What if the cry is really this: "Why did you leave us alone for so long? Why did you not correct us sooner?"

Anyone who has watched a bad habit grow understands the concern. A small compromise can become a pattern. A pattern can become a way of life. Left unchecked, we begin to mistake our condition for normal. In this sense, discipline can be a form of mercy. A loving parent corrects a child not because the child is rejected, but because the child matters. The absence of correction is not always kindness. Sometimes it is simply abandonment to the consequences of our own choices.

This helps explain why trials and difficulties can play an important role in spiritual growth. Challenges reveal what is already present within us. Patience is discovered when patience is tested. Courage is revealed when fear appears. Faithfulness becomes visible only when there is a reason to give up. The purpose is not that God needs this information. The deeper purpose is that we need to see ourselves clearly. Much of what lies hidden within us remains invisible until life brings it into the light.

There is another reason this self-knowledge matters. A person who has never recognized a weakness rarely appreciates help. Imagine someone recovering from a serious illness without ever realizing how sick they were. They would have little gratitude for the physician who restored them. In the same way, if we never recognize our limitations, we may begin to believe that every good thing we possess originated from ourselves. We mistake grace for personal achievement.

This, according to the argument, is the hidden danger of pride. The greatest threat is not failure but success that convinces us we no longer need anyone. When people forget the source of their gifts, they begin to treat borrowed treasures as personal possessions. The story of spiritual downfall often begins with the quiet assumption that our wisdom, strength, or goodness belongs entirely to us. What was received is mistaken for something earned.

For this reason, growth often begins with humility. Before we can be filled, we must recognize what is lacking. Before we can receive healing, we must admit our need for it. The person who understands both their weakness and the help that has been given to them stands on firmer ground than the person who imagines they have accomplished everything alone. Gratitude flourishes where self-knowledge and grace meet, and it is there that genuine spiritual maturity begins.
