## 26 — Can a Soul Exist Without a Body?

> *"It seems very difficult and well-nigh impossible."*

When people imagine spiritual perfection, they often picture freedom from everything physical. The body is seen as temporary, while the soul is thought to be the thing that truly matters. From this perspective, the highest state would be one in which the soul finally leaves matter behind altogether.

But is that actually possible?

To explore the question, consider what would follow if a rational being could exist entirely without a body. If souls could live perfectly well on their own, then matter might be little more than a temporary tool. Bodies would serve a purpose for a season and then disappear forever once that purpose had been fulfilled. In that case, bodily existence would be an interruption rather than an enduring part of creation.

Yet another possibility presents itself. What if embodiment is not merely a temporary arrangement but something woven into the very life of created beings? If that were true, then bodies would not be discarded at the end of the journey. They would be transformed. The question would shift from whether we have bodies to what kind of bodies we have.

This is the direction the argument takes. God alone exists entirely beyond all bodily limitation. The Father, Son, and Holy Spirit need nothing outside themselves in order to exist. Created beings are different. Rational creatures may grow in wisdom, holiness, and freedom, but they remain creatures. Their existence is always expressed through some form of embodiment.

That does not mean every body is the same. Matter is remarkably flexible. A seed becomes a tree. Water becomes cloud and rain. Food becomes flesh and bone. Everywhere in nature we find transformation rather than permanence of form. In the same way, the bodies appropriate to different stages of existence may differ enormously. The dense and fragile bodies of earthly life need not be the final form of embodiment. Scripture speaks of "spiritual bodies," suggesting a mode of existence in which matter is no longer a burden but a fitting expression of a life fully aligned with God.

Think of a musician and an instrument. The musician is not the instrument, yet the music becomes audible only through some means of expression. The better the instrument, the more beautifully the music is revealed. In a similar way, the soul may not be identical with the body, yet it appears to require some form through which its life is expressed. Spiritual growth, then, is not about becoming less real or less embodied. It is about becoming more fully what we were meant to be.

The discussion ends with humility. These questions reach beyond ordinary experience, and certainty is difficult. Some mysteries can only be approached through patient reflection and careful attention. Yet the central insight remains clear: the destiny of creation is not annihilation but transformation. The goal is not to escape existence but to allow every aspect of existence—even embodiment itself—to be reshaped by divine life. What is imperfect is not discarded. It is refined, renewed, and brought into closer harmony with its source.
