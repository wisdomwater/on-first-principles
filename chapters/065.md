## 65 — The Timing of Mercy

> *"He helps them by not helping them, since this course is for their profit."*

One of the most startling ideas is the suggestion that immediate forgiveness is not always the greatest possible good. That sounds strange to modern ears. We tend to assume that if healing is available, it should happen at once. If someone is ready to turn around, why would God ever delay the process?

The argument begins with an observation about human nature. People often learn only what they are prepared to value. When a gift comes too easily, it can be taken for granted. A person who repeatedly escapes the consequences of destructive choices may never develop a deep hatred for the destruction itself. Instead, they may come to believe that the damage is minor, the cure is simple, and repentance is something that can always be postponed until later.

Imagine someone recovering from a serious addiction. If every relapse carried no real cost, they might never fully confront the depth of the problem. They could drift in and out of recovery without ever developing the lasting resolve needed for genuine freedom. Sometimes it is only after a person sees clearly where a path leads that they become ready to leave it behind for good. The goal is not merely temporary improvement but lasting transformation.

This is how the difficult passages about parables are being understood. The people who hear only in riddles are not being permanently excluded from truth. They are still hearing something. They remain near enough to receive hints, glimpses, and invitations. Others seem even farther away, not hearing at all. Yet even this distance is not interpreted as final rejection. The suggestion is that different people are being reached at different stages of readiness, according to what will ultimately produce the deepest and most enduring change.

What makes this argument remarkable is its refusal to see anyone as abandoned forever. Even those who appear farthest away remain within the scope of divine care. Their healing may be delayed, their lessons may take longer, and their path may be more difficult, but the larger purpose remains restorative. The timing differs because the condition differs. A physician treats a minor wound differently than a deeply rooted disease. The goal in both cases is health, but the process is not identical.

Of course, there is a great deal of mystery here. We cannot see all the factors that shape a human life. We do not know the full story of another person's struggles, choices, wounds, or opportunities. That is why certainty is dangerous when discussing questions of judgment, grace, and spiritual growth. We see only fragments. The larger pattern remains hidden from us.

The heart of the argument is not that suffering or delay is good in itself. It is that divine wisdom may sometimes value lasting healing over immediate relief. What appears to us as absence may be preparation. What looks like silence may be patience. And what seems like a closed door today may simply be a door that has not yet reached its appointed time to open.
