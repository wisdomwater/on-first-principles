## 55 — More Than Your Circumstances

> *“Things which happen from without are not in our power, but to use them well or badly… this lies within our power.”*

A common way of avoiding responsibility is to place the entire burden of our actions somewhere else. We blame our circumstances. We blame our upbringing. We blame our temperament. We blame the people around us. We point to desires, habits, or influences and say, “How could I have acted differently?”

There is truth hidden within these explanations. Circumstances matter. Habits matter. The company we keep matters. No one develops in isolation. Yet if these things completely determined who we become, then genuine change would be impossible. A cruel person would remain cruel forever. A selfish person could never become generous. A fearful person could never become courageous.

But experience tells a different story.

Think about the people you have known over the years. Some who began life reckless and impulsive became wise and disciplined. Others who once seemed gentle and honorable drifted into bitterness or corruption. Entire lives can change direction. Sometimes a single insight, a wise mentor, a difficult hardship, or a profound awakening becomes the turning point that alters the course of a person’s character.

If human beings were nothing more than the products of their constitution or environment, such transformations would make little sense. We might explain small adjustments, but not the remarkable reversals that sometimes occur. Yet history and ordinary experience are filled with stories of people overcoming destructive habits, reshaping their character, and becoming someone very different from who they once were.

This does not mean that external influences are unimportant. Quite the opposite. Words can inspire. Friendships can elevate or degrade. Education can awaken possibilities that would otherwise remain dormant. We are constantly being influenced by what happens around us. The question is whether those influences possess absolute authority over us. The answer offered here is no. They present opportunities, pressures, and invitations, but they do not make the final decision.

The power of reason allows us to evaluate what confronts us. We can strengthen some influences and resist others. We can choose which voices deserve our trust. We can cooperate with habits that move us toward wisdom or surrender ourselves to habits that lead elsewhere. The very fact that human beings can grow, reform, decline, repent, learn, and change reveals that something within us remains capable of directing the course of our lives.

For this reason, the call to live well is meaningful. The ancient scriptures repeatedly speak in the language of choice: choose life, choose what is good, listen, walk, turn, obey. Such language would be empty if human beings lacked the ability to respond. We do not control every circumstance that enters our lives, but we are not merely the sum of those circumstances. Between what happens to us and what we become stands the mysterious and powerful capacity to choose how we will respond.
