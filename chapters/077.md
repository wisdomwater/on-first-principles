## 77 — The Voices We Learn to Trust

> *"The soul of man, while in the body, can admit different energies."*

No one forms their understanding of life entirely alone.

From our earliest years we are influenced by parents, teachers, friends, books, traditions, and cultures. Ideas enter our minds long before we consciously examine them. Some help us see more clearly. Others quietly distort our vision. Over time these influences shape not only what we think but also what we become.

This raises an important question. Why do certain people seem drawn toward wisdom, compassion, and truth, while others become increasingly captive to destructive ways of thinking? The answer cannot simply be intelligence. Brilliant people can be deeply mistaken. Nor can it be education alone. History contains many educated individuals who used their knowledge for harmful ends.

The deeper issue is receptivity. We gradually become open to the influences that match the direction of our lives. A person who repeatedly chooses honesty becomes more capable of recognizing truth. A person who continually nourishes resentment becomes more susceptible to bitterness. What we practice creates pathways within us, making some voices easier to hear and others easier to ignore.

Think of a musical instrument. When properly tuned, it resonates naturally with certain notes. When neglected or damaged, those same notes sound distorted. The human soul functions in a similar way. Our habits, desires, and commitments affect what we are able to receive. Some influences awaken courage, humility, and love. Others strengthen pride, fear, envy, and self-deception.

This helps explain why error is often more complicated than simple malice. People do not usually embrace falsehood because they consciously desire deception. More often they become convinced that their particular way of seeing the world is correct. Once that happens, they naturally seek to persuade others. A mistaken idea can spread not only through bad intentions but also through sincere conviction.

Yet the opposite is equally true. Just as harmful influences can shape a life, so can beneficial ones. Every act of honesty strengthens our capacity for truth. Every movement toward compassion increases our ability to recognize goodness. The soul is continually being formed by what it welcomes and what it resists.

For this reason, wisdom requires more than collecting information. It requires attending carefully to the influences we allow to guide us. Not every voice deserves equal trust. Some lead toward greater freedom, clarity, and love. Others lead toward confusion, division, and self-absorption. The task of spiritual maturity is learning the difference.

The ideas that shape us today become the character we carry tomorrow. What we repeatedly listen to, we slowly become.
