## 80 — What Was God Doing Before the World?

> *"The creation itself also shall be delivered from the bondage of corruption."*

Most of us assume the world has always been here. Even when we speak of a beginning, it is difficult to imagine what existed before it. Our minds naturally think in terms of time, sequence, and events. We ask what happened first, and then what happened before that.

Yet this creates a puzzle. If the world had a beginning, what was God doing beforehand?

At first the question seems troublesome. If God is eternally good, then goodness must always be active. If God is eternally creative, then creativity cannot suddenly appear at a particular moment. It would be strange to imagine an infinite God waiting through endless ages before finally deciding to create something.

The first step toward resolving the difficulty is to recognize that the world itself is not eternal. Everything around us bears the marks of change. Things come into being, grow, decline, and pass away. Scripture speaks of creation in the same way. The world has a beginning, and it is moving toward a future transformation. What begins is not ultimate. What changes is not permanent.

But this does not mean that God's activity began when the world began. To imagine that would make God's life dependent upon creation. Instead, the better conclusion is that God's creative work is far larger than the history of this present world. The visible universe that we inhabit may be only one chapter in a much greater story.

Think of a teacher entering a classroom. The lesson begins for the students when the teacher walks through the door, but the teacher did not begin to exist at that moment. Nor did the teacher's wisdom suddenly appear with the start of the class. The lesson has a beginning, but the teacher's life extends beyond it.

In the same way, creation may have a beginning without requiring God to have a beginning. The world enters the stage of existence at a particular moment, yet the divine life from which it springs is eternal. The changing order of creation unfolds within time, while God remains beyond the limitations of time.

This perspective also changes how we think about endings. If the world has a purpose, then its end need not mean annihilation. The ending of one chapter often prepares the beginning of another. A seed disappears so that a tree may emerge. Childhood passes away so that maturity can arise. Transformation frequently looks like an ending when viewed from within the process itself.

The deeper question, then, is not what God was doing before the world. It is whether we have imagined God too narrowly. If the Creator is truly eternal, then divine activity cannot be confined to the brief span of history that we happen to inhabit. The world may have a beginning and an end, but the life and goodness from which it comes extend beyond both.
