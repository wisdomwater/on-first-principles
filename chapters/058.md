## 58 — One Action, Different Hearts

> *“God has mercy on one and hardens another by one and the same operation.”*

The question now becomes sharper. If God is good and just, how can Scripture say that God hardened Pharaoh’s heart? If Pharaoh became disobedient because God made him so, then how could Pharaoh be blamed? A judge cannot justly condemn someone for doing what the judge himself caused him to do.

This is why we must be careful. It is not enough to repeat the words, “God hardened Pharaoh’s heart,” as though the phrase explains itself. We have to ask what kind of hardening is meant. Does God directly create evil in Pharaoh? Does God force him into rebellion? Or is something more subtle happening?

A helpful way forward is to notice that the same action can produce different effects depending on what receives it. The sun shines with one warmth, yet that warmth softens wax and hardens clay. The difference is not in the sun but in the material. The same heat that makes one substance pliable makes another more rigid. In this way, a single operation can lead to mercy in one case and hardening in another.

So it may be with the works of God. God acts. God reveals. God sends signs, warnings, judgments, and opportunities for repentance. To a receptive heart, these become mercy. They awaken humility. They call forth change. They soften what has grown resistant. But to a proud or stubborn heart, the same divine action can produce greater resistance. The light does not become darkness, but the diseased eye may experience light as pain.

This protects both truths at once. God remains good and just, because God is not creating evil within the person. God is revealing, warning, calling, and acting for a larger purpose. Yet the human response remains real. Pharaoh’s hardening is not the result of God pouring wickedness into him, but of Pharaoh’s own resistance meeting the pressure of divine revelation. The more God’s power is shown, the more Pharaoh’s inner refusal becomes exposed.

This also explains why the story matters beyond Pharaoh. His resistance becomes the stage on which God’s deliverance is revealed for many. Yet Pharaoh is not reduced to a puppet. His own unwillingness is still involved. God’s action brings to the surface what is already present in him, and under that pressure his heart becomes harder rather than humbler.

The same mystery appears in ordinary life. A correction can make one person grateful and another defensive. A hardship can deepen one soul and embitter another. A truth spoken in love can heal the humble and offend the proud. The event may be the same, but the response reveals the condition of the heart. Divine mercy is not absent when a heart hardens. Sometimes mercy is shining with full force, and the tragedy is that the heart turns that light into resistance.
