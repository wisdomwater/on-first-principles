## 36 — Iron in the Fire

> *“He who is joined to the Lord is one spirit.”*

How can the infinite God truly unite himself with a human life?

This is the question that naturally follows once we have allowed ourselves to feel the wonder of the incarnation. It is one thing to say that God became human. It is another to ask how such a union could take place without destroying either God's divinity or humanity itself.

To approach this mystery, consider something that appears throughout human experience: the power of love to unite.

The people we love shape us. Their hopes become our concerns. Their joys become our joys. Over time, two lives can become so closely joined that it becomes difficult to think of one without the other. Love does not erase individuality, yet it creates a profound participation in another person's life.

Now imagine a love unlike any other.

Imagine a soul so completely devoted to God that nothing within it turns away. No divided loyalties. No competing desires. No hidden resistance. Every thought, affection, and intention directed toward the divine. Such a soul would not merely believe in God or obey God from a distance. It would live in continual union with God.

This is what occurred in Christ.

The humanity of Jesus was not different in kind from our humanity. It possessed the same rational nature found in every human soul. Yet from the very beginning it remained perfectly united with the divine Word. Through an unbroken love for truth, goodness, and God himself, that union became complete and inseparable.

Think of a piece of iron placed into a blazing fire. At first the iron and the fire are clearly distinct. Yet if the iron remains in the flames long enough, the fire penetrates every part of it. Eventually the iron glows with the fire's brightness. It radiates the fire's heat. Touch it, and you experience fire rather than iron. The iron has not ceased to be iron, yet it has become so permeated by the fire that the two seem inseparable.

This is how we should think about Christ.

The humanity remains truly human. The divinity remains truly divine. Yet the union between them is so complete that wherever Christ acts, the presence of God shines through. His thoughts, his words, his compassion, his obedience, and his love all reveal the divine life that fills him.

There is another reason this image matters.

Christ is not merely an exception to humanity. This principle appears elsewhere in Scripture: "He who is joined to the Lord is one spirit." The union found uniquely and perfectly in Christ reveals the destiny toward which all spiritual life moves. Not that we become God by nature, but that we become so filled with God's life that his goodness shines through us.

The iron never becomes the fire's source. Yet it can become so filled with fire that it glows with the fire's own brightness.

Perhaps this is why the incarnation matters so deeply. It reveals not only who Christ is, but what humanity itself can become when it is wholly surrendered to the life of God.
