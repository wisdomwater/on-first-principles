## 108 — The Heavenly Jerusalem

> *“The Jerusalem which is above is free, which is our mother.”*

When most people hear the word Jerusalem, they think of a city. They imagine walls, streets, markets, temples, and the long history attached to a particular place on earth. Scripture certainly speaks about that city. Yet the New Testament introduces a surprising idea: there is also a Jerusalem "above."

At first this sounds strange. How can a city exist in heaven? But the question becomes easier if we remember that scripture often uses visible realities to reveal invisible ones. We speak of a university not merely as a collection of buildings but as a community, a culture, and a shared life. In the same way, Jerusalem may signify more than a geographical location. It can represent a spiritual reality—a community gathered around the presence of God.

This insight changes how many prophetic passages are read. Throughout scripture, Jerusalem is threatened, conquered, restored, exalted, and filled with glory. If we think only of the earthly city, many of these promises seem difficult to reconcile with history. Yet if Jerusalem also symbolizes the spiritual community of God's people, the language begins to open into wider horizons.

The same is true of the land surrounding Jerusalem. The prophets speak of nations, cities, invasions, victories, and restorations. These events belong to history, yet they may also portray realities unfolding within the soul. Every person knows something of exile and return. We know what it is like to become divided within ourselves, to lose our way, and to seek restoration. The language of nations and cities can become a language for the inner life.

Consider how naturally this happens in ordinary speech. We talk about "building a life," "defending our boundaries," or "finding our center." None of these are descriptions of literal construction projects. They are images that help us understand inward realities. Scripture often operates with a similar richness. The visible city becomes a picture of an invisible community and an inward condition.

This does not diminish the importance of the earthly Jerusalem. Rather, it allows the city to serve a larger purpose. Its history becomes a sign of truths that reach beyond one place and one people. The city becomes an icon through which the reader glimpses something eternal.

When read this way, the prophecies concerning Jerusalem become more than predictions about a location on a map. They become revelations about the destiny of souls and the gathering of humanity into communion with God. The earthly city remains, but it points beyond itself toward a greater Jerusalem—the city that exists wherever God's life is fully known and shared.
