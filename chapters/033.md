## 33 — The Medicine of Justice

> *“Neither goodness without justice nor justice without goodness can describe the dignity of the divine nature.”*

When we suffer consequences for our actions, it is easy to feel as though justice and kindness are opposites. Justice seems to take away. Kindness seems to give. One feels painful; the other feels pleasant. Because of this, many people assume that a truly good person would simply overlook faults and spare others from hardship whenever possible.

But consider how often growth comes through things we would never choose for ourselves.

A physician may prescribe a painful treatment. A teacher may correct a student. A parent may impose a consequence that a child dislikes. In each case, discomfort is present. Yet the goal is not harm. The goal is healing. What feels severe in the moment may actually be an expression of care.

This is the mistake being challenged here. Some people imagine that justice means giving suffering to the wicked, while goodness means giving pleasant things to everyone. If that were true, then goodness and justice would always be pulling in opposite directions. One would forgive while the other would punish. One would heal while the other would condemn.

But that understanding is too shallow.

A deeper view sees justice not as revenge but as restoration. The purpose of correction is not simply to inflict pain. Its purpose is to set things right. When a surgeon cuts away disease, the knife is not acting against health but in service of it. In the same way, what appears to be divine judgment may be aimed at healing realities we cannot yet see.

This is why the distinction between a merely good God and a merely just God ultimately breaks down. If justice is truly a virtue, then it must itself be good. Otherwise justice would be either evil or morally meaningless. Yet no one believes that genuine justice is evil. The very fact that we praise justice shows that we recognize it as a form of goodness.

The argument works in the other direction as well. Goodness that never confronts what is destructive eventually ceases to be good. A goodness that allows harm to flourish unchecked is not compassion but neglect. To care for someone is sometimes to comfort them and sometimes to challenge them. The action changes, but the love behind it remains the same.

Seen this way, many of the difficult stories in Scripture take on a different character. Instead of asking whether God is being kind or being just, we begin asking whether these are two different names for the same deeper reality. What if divine justice is not the opposite of divine goodness but one of the ways goodness works?

The challenge, then, is not deciding whether God is loving or just. The challenge is expanding our understanding of both words. We often call something good only when it feels pleasant, and just only when it feels severe. Yet the highest form of goodness may include correction, and the highest form of justice may be motivated entirely by love.

When goodness and justice are finally understood in their fullness, they no longer stand apart. They become two faces of the same reality—the unwavering commitment to bring every creature toward what is true, healthy, and whole.
