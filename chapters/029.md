## 29 — Beyond the Ages

> *"When all things are no longer in an age, but God is all and in all."*

When we think about eternity, we usually imagine more time. We picture years stretching into centuries, centuries into millennia, and millennia into endless ages. But what if eternity is not merely an endless extension of time? What if it is something deeper?

The discussion begins with a simple observation. Scripture speaks not only of "this age" but of "ages to come." This suggests that the present world is not the whole story. There are further stages of growth, further opportunities for learning, and further movements in God's work of restoration. Yet the more we reflect on this, the more we will wonder whether even these future ages point beyond themselves to something greater.

Think of a student moving through school. There are many grades, many classrooms, and many seasons of learning. Each stage is important, but none is the final goal. The purpose of education is not to remain forever in one grade after another. The grades exist to prepare the student for maturity. In the same way, the ages may be understood as stages within a larger journey whose destination lies beyond them.

This possibility is hinted at in the vision of God becoming "all in all." As long as there are ages, there are still processes unfolding. There is still movement, learning, growth, and restoration. But the final goal is not endless process. It is complete participation in divine life. The journey exists for the sake of union. The school exists for the sake of wisdom. The road exists for the sake of arrival.

What do we mean when we say the word "world." We often think of a world as a planet or a physical universe. Yet the word can also mean an ordered arrangement, a realm, or even an ornament. This opens the door to a larger vision of reality. Perhaps the visible world is only one level within a far greater order. Just as a city contains neighborhoods, and a nation contains many cities, the universe itself may contain dimensions of existence beyond those immediately available to our senses.

Yet there is an important caution. These realities should not be imagined as mere fantasies or abstract ideas floating somewhere beyond the stars. The goal is not to escape into an imaginary realm. Rather, the suggestion is that reality may be richer and more layered than our present experience allows us to perceive. The visible world may be only the outermost surface of a much larger creation sustained by the same divine wisdom.

This leads to a distinction between the visible and the unseen. Something unseen is not necessarily unreal. In fact, many of the most important realities in life are unseen. Love cannot be weighed. Wisdom cannot be measured with a ruler. Justice cannot be photographed. Yet their effects shape entire lives. In a similar way, there may be dimensions of God's creation that are not currently visible to us but are no less real for being hidden.

The invitation is not to speculate endlessly about celestial worlds or invisible heavens. It is to remember that creation points beyond itself. Every age points toward something greater than the age. Every world points toward something greater than the world. The final hope is not merely to inhabit a better place but to enter more deeply into the life of God, until all the stages of the journey have fulfilled their purpose and God is truly all in all.
