## 59 — The Same Sun, Different Results

> *“The goodness and impartiality of the rain visits all lands alike.”*

When something good produces different results in different people, we are often tempted to assume the source itself must be different. If one person grows wiser through adversity while another becomes bitter, we may wonder whether they experienced different circumstances. Yet often the same event can lead in opposite directions.

This observation lies at the heart of the question about Pharaoh. If God's presence softened some hearts and hardened Pharaoh's, does that mean God was acting mercifully toward one person and harshly toward another? Or could the same divine action have produced different results because of the condition of the hearts that received it?

Imagine rain falling upon a field. The rain does not choose where to be fruitful and where to be barren. It falls equally upon the ground. Yet one field yields grain, while another produces only thorns and weeds. The difference is not in the rain. The difference lies in the condition of the soil. Land that has been cultivated responds differently than land left neglected and overgrown.

The same principle can be seen in the warmth of the sun. The sun softens wax and hardens clay. Yet no one would say that the sun possesses two conflicting intentions. It shines with a single heat. The opposite effects arise from the nature of the materials exposed to it. The wax yields. The clay resists. One becomes softer while the other grows firmer.

This, Origen suggests, is how we should understand God's dealings with human beings. God acts with goodness, patience, and generosity. The divine purpose is not to create evil in anyone. Yet when that goodness encounters different hearts, different results emerge. A humble person may become more receptive. A proud person may become more stubborn. The same revelation that awakens repentance in one person may provoke resistance in another.

Seen this way, Pharaoh's hardening does not mean that God implanted wickedness within him. Rather, God's signs, warnings, and mighty works exposed what was already present. Each encounter gave Pharaoh another opportunity to yield, but instead his resistance deepened. The light shining upon him revealed the condition of his heart. What softened others only made him more determined to oppose what he had already chosen.

This insight reaches far beyond Pharaoh. Every blessing carries the same possibility. Success can produce gratitude or arrogance. Correction can lead to growth or defensiveness. Truth can awaken humility or strengthen pride. The difference is not always found in what we receive but in how we receive it. God's grace may fall like rain upon every field and shine like sunlight upon every surface, yet the fruit that emerges depends in part upon the condition of the soil. Divine goodness is constant. The response to that goodness remains a matter of the heart.
