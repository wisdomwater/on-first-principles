## 15 — One Family of Rational Beings

> *“Every rational creature is therefore susceptible of praise and of blame.”*

When we look at the world, we naturally sort beings into categories. Some appear exalted, others fallen. Some seem wise, others foolish. Some inspire admiration, while others provoke fear. Scripture itself speaks of angels, powers, principalities, rulers, demons, and human beings, each carrying different names and different functions.

But what if these differences are not the whole story?

The question before us is deceptively simple. Did God create some rational beings inherently good and incapable of falling, while creating others inherently corrupt and incapable of rising? Did some begin with a privileged nature while others were destined from the start for lower places? Or is there another explanation for the vast diversity we observe among rational creatures?

Scripture describes a remarkable variety of spiritual orders. There are angels who serve and guide. There are powers and dominions whose exact functions remain mysterious. There are hostile forces opposed to goodness. There are human beings caught in the middle, capable of both virtue and vice. The sheer number of names suggests a universe far more complex than ordinary experience reveals.

Yet beneath all these distinctions lies a common feature. Every being under discussion is rational. Every one possesses, in some measure, the capacity to understand, choose, respond, and direct itself. This shared capacity becomes the key to the entire argument. For wherever reason exists, freedom exists as well. And wherever freedom exists, both growth and decline become possible.

Notice how different this perspective is from a world divided into fixed classes. We often assume that what something is now is what it has always been. We see a saint and imagine they were destined for holiness. We see a person trapped in destructive patterns and assume that is simply their nature. But experience teaches otherwise. People change. They mature, regress, awaken, forget, recover, and transform. Character is not a static possession but something continually shaped through countless choices.

This is true to the entire realm of rational existence. Praise and blame make sense only if freedom is real. We do not praise a stone for falling downward or blame a flame for rising upward. Those actions follow necessity. Praise and blame belong only to beings capable of choosing. Therefore, whenever Scripture speaks of reward, correction, faithfulness, rebellion, growth, or decline, it assumes a freedom that belongs to rational creatures themselves.

This insight becomes the foundation for everything that follows. The differences we observe among rational beings cannot be explained simply by saying that God made some good and others bad. Such a view would undermine both justice and responsibility. Instead, the diversity of conditions points toward a history of choices, movements, and responses that have shaped each being's present state.

Beneath all the visible distinctions, rational creatures share a common dignity. Angels, humans, and even fallen powers are not fundamentally different kinds of reality but are members of a single family of rational beings. The mystery is not why some were created higher and others lower. The mystery is how beings sharing the same gift of freedom came to occupy such different places along the journey.
