## 43 — Why the World Is So Different

> *"The Creator granted to the minds created by him the power of free and voluntary movement."*

When we look at the world, one fact is impossible to ignore: nothing seems evenly distributed.

Some people are born into comfort, others into poverty. Some seem naturally gifted, while others struggle. Some are drawn toward wisdom and compassion from an early age; others spend much of their lives lost in confusion or destructive habits. The same diversity appears throughout creation. There are countless differences in ability, circumstance, opportunity, and character.

The question is simple but profound: Where did all this variety come from?

One possible answer is that God simply made creatures unequal from the beginning. But that creates a difficult problem. If God arbitrarily assigns greater blessings to some and lesser blessings to others, then the differences we see would reflect favoritism rather than justice. Here a different possibility is proposed. Imagine that all rational beings began from the same divine generosity. They were created good, not because goodness belonged to them by nature, but because it was freely given by their Creator.

Yet a gift can be either cherished or neglected.

A musician may receive a finely crafted instrument. If he practices faithfully, the gift becomes increasingly his own. If he ignores it, the instrument falls silent and gathers dust. In the same way, the good that was given to rational beings was meant to be embraced freely. Love, wisdom, and communion with God were not intended to be imposed. They were meant to be chosen. Because freedom was real, movement was possible. A creature could remain turned toward the good or gradually drift away from it.

This drift, we are told, did not begin through some dramatic act of rebellion. It began through neglect. Weariness. A loss of attention. A failure to value what had been received. Evil, in this view, is not a thing created by God. It is what happens when goodness is abandoned. Darkness is not an independent force; it is what remains when light is absent. In the same way, the farther a being moves from the good, the more deeply it experiences what we call evil.

From this perspective, the astonishing diversity of the world begins to make sense. The differences we see among creatures are not evidence of a divided Creator but of countless different responses to freedom. Some remain closer to the divine source. Others have wandered farther away. The universe becomes a vast landscape shaped by the many directions taken by rational beings over time.

Whether one accepts every part of this explanation or not, the insight at its heart remains powerful. The most important movements in life are often not forced upon us from the outside. They arise from the countless small choices of attention, desire, and commitment that shape who we become. We are always moving toward the good or away from it. And those movements, however small they may seem today, gradually create the world we inhabit tomorrow.
