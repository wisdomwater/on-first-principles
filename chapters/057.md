## 57 — The Problem with Fixed Natures

> *“Whom he will he hardeneth.”*

Once we begin talking about freedom, difficult questions quickly arise. If human beings truly possess the ability to choose, what do we make of passages that seem to say otherwise? What do we do with statements about God hardening Pharaoh's heart, showing mercy to some and not others, or shaping one person for honor and another for dishonor?

These questions are not new. Long before our own debates about fate, predestination, and free will, people were wrestling with them. Some reached a radical conclusion. They argued that human beings belong to different kinds of natures. Some are naturally destined for salvation. Others are naturally destined for destruction. Just as a tree can only produce the fruit proper to its kind, they claimed that certain souls are incapable of becoming good while others are incapable of ultimately being lost.

At first glance, this idea seems to explain difficult passages. If Pharaoh's heart was hardened, perhaps it was because he belonged to a class of people created for that very purpose. If some receive mercy and others do not, perhaps the difference lies in their nature rather than their choices.

Yet this explanation creates larger problems than the ones it solves.

Consider Pharaoh. If he was utterly incapable of responding to God, why would his heart need to be hardened at all? Hardening only makes sense if there is something present that can become harder. No one speaks of hardening stone into stone. The very language assumes a capacity for response that is being resisted or redirected. If Pharaoh were incapable of obedience from the beginning, the entire story would become strangely unnecessary.

The same difficulty appears whenever Scripture urges people to repent, choose, listen, obey, or turn from evil. Such appeals only make sense if change is possible. Why warn someone against a path they are incapable of avoiding? Why invite someone into a life they are incapable of entering? The language of exhortation, correction, and transformation assumes that human beings are not locked into a fixed destiny from birth.

Ordinary experience points in the same direction. People change. The selfish become generous. The cruel become compassionate. The faithful become faithless. Character develops over time. If human beings belonged to unalterable categories of the saved and the lost, such transformations would be difficult to explain. Yet they happen constantly. Life itself testifies that people are capable of becoming something other than what they presently are.

Before attempting to explain what it means for God to harden a heart, we must first avoid an interpretation that destroys human responsibility altogether. The stories of Scripture assume that people can respond to God, resist God, cooperate with grace, or turn away from it. Whatever these difficult passages ultimately mean, they cannot mean that human beings are merely actors reciting lines written into their nature from the beginning. The drama of Scripture depends upon the possibility of a genuine response, and without that possibility the entire conversation about good, evil, judgment, and mercy begins to unravel.
