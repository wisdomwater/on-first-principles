## 64 — Helped by Not Being Helped

> *"In his goodness, God delays the healing of such persons and, if I may say so, heals them by not healing them."*

Some of the most difficult passages in Scripture are those that seem to suggest God withholds understanding from people. Why would truth ever be concealed? Why would someone be prevented from seeing clearly, especially if seeing clearly might lead to repentance and forgiveness?

At first, the question appears troubling. If understanding leads to healing, shouldn't healing be offered immediately? Yet there is a deeper issue beneath the surface. Not every cure is lasting simply because it is quick. A person may abandon a destructive habit for a season, only to return to it as soon as the pressure passes. Outward change can happen before inward readiness. In such cases, what looks like healing may only be a pause in the disease.

Think of a physician treating a recurring illness. If the symptoms disappear before the underlying condition is addressed, the patient may assume the problem is solved. Feeling secure, they return to the very habits that caused the sickness in the first place. The temporary relief becomes part of the problem because it creates the illusion of health. A deeper cure sometimes requires a longer process, one that reaches beneath the surface and addresses the roots rather than merely the symptoms.

This is the principle being applied to the difficult sayings of Jesus about speaking in parables. The argument is not that God delights in keeping people ignorant. Rather, there are moments when a person is not yet prepared to receive a truth in its fullness. If profound spiritual realities are grasped too quickly, without the maturity needed to sustain them, they may be misunderstood, discarded, or treated carelessly. What is precious can be lost when it is received before a person is ready to treasure it.

Seen from this perspective, delay can become a form of mercy. What appears to be withholding may actually be preparation. A teacher does not begin with advanced lessons on the first day of instruction. A parent does not place adult responsibilities upon a child. Wisdom grows in stages. Each step prepares the way for the next. What seems like concealment may simply be patience working toward a deeper and more enduring understanding.

Underlying this entire argument is a remarkable confidence in divine wisdom. Human beings see only the present moment, but God sees the entire journey. We often judge situations by what would bring immediate relief, immediate clarity, or immediate success. Yet the greater concern may be what will lead to lasting transformation years later. A short path is not always the safest path.

This does not mean every delay is good or that every unanswered question is part of a hidden plan. Rather, it invites humility about the limits of our perspective. There are times when we desperately want an answer, an insight, or a breakthrough, and none seems to come. In those moments, it may be worth considering that preparation is itself part of the healing. The gardener who waits before planting and the physician who refuses a superficial cure may appear inactive, yet both are working toward something deeper than immediate results: a transformation that endures.
