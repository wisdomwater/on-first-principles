## 116 — Why the Soul Endures

> *“All the ends of the earth shall remember and be turned unto the Lord.”*

Why should we believe that the soul endures? Why think that our deepest self is not simply extinguished when the body passes away?

One answer begins with a simple observation: things that share in the same reality tend to belong to the same kind of existence. Every eye receives light. Every ear receives sound. Though some eyes see more clearly than others and some ears hear more sharply, they all participate in the same reality and therefore share a common nature.

Now carry that idea into the realm of the spirit. Every rational being participates, in some measure, in wisdom, truth, and understanding. Human souls do this. Angels do this. Every mind that perceives truth receives its light from the same divine source. If these different beings share in the same spiritual light, then there must be a profound kinship among them. They belong to the same family of existence.

From this, a remarkable conclusion follows. If the source of that light is eternal, and if rational beings are capable of participating in that eternal reality, then their existence cannot be merely temporary. A mind capable of knowing truth, seeking goodness, and receiving God is not like a spark that flares briefly and disappears. It has been fashioned for something greater. The very capacity to receive the eternal points toward an enduring destiny.

This does not mean that all souls receive the divine life equally. Just as one eye sees more clearly than another, one person may be more open to wisdom, love, and truth than another. Growth remains possible. Some souls receive the light dimly, others brightly. Yet the differences are differences of participation, not of nature. The capacity remains present in all.

Indeed, it would seem strange to say that a mind capable of knowing God could simply vanish into nothingness. Even when a person wanders far from truth, there remains something within that remembers. Beneath confusion, failure, and forgetfulness lie what might be called seeds of restoration. A longing for goodness survives. A hunger for truth remains. A faint memory of home continues to whisper. These seeds may sleep for a time, but they are never wholly destroyed.

This is why spiritual growth is often described as remembrance rather than mere acquisition. We do not become something entirely foreign to ourselves. We awaken to what we were created for from the beginning. The image of God within us may become obscured, but it is not erased. Through wisdom, virtue, and love, that image gradually shines more clearly.

The path of the soul, then, is not a journey toward annihilation but toward fulfillment. We were created with the ability to know truth, love goodness, and participate in the life of God. Those capacities are not accidents. They are signs of our origin and hints of our destiny. The divine image within us points beyond the limits of this present life toward a future in which what is now dimly perceived may one day be seen with far greater clarity.
