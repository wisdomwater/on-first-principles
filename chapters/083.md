## 83 — From Image to Likeness

> *"God made man in his own image."*

When people speak about the purpose of life, they often describe it in terms of success, happiness, knowledge, or fulfillment. But there is an older and deeper answer: the purpose of life is to become like God. Not to become God in essence, but to grow into a fuller participation in God's wisdom, goodness, love, and life.

This idea begins with a curious detail in the creation story. We are told that humanity was created in God's image, yet the text does not immediately say that humanity possessed God's likeness in its fullness. The distinction may seem small, but it points to something profound. The image is given at the beginning. The likeness is something toward which we grow. In other words, human beings are created with the potential for divine resemblance, but that potential must be developed through the journey of life.

Think of a child born with a remarkable gift. The capacity is present from the first day, but the maturity of that gift lies in the future. A gifted musician must still learn to play. A gifted athlete must still train. In the same way, the image of God within us is a beginning rather than a finish line. We are created with the ability to become what we were intended to be, but we must freely participate in that transformation.

This helps explain why spiritual growth matters. The goal is not merely following rules or avoiding mistakes. The deeper purpose is the gradual shaping of the soul into harmony with God's character. Every act of love, every movement toward truth, every victory over selfishness contributes to that process. We become like what we continually seek. If we seek goodness, goodness begins to take root within us. If we seek wisdom, wisdom slowly reshapes our minds. The journey is not about earning God's favor but about growing into the likeness that was always intended for us.

The promise of this transformation reaches beyond the present life. Scripture speaks of a future in which humanity will see more clearly what it is destined to become. We know only fragments now. We see ourselves as unfinished people, carrying both beauty and brokenness. Yet the hope held before us is that the image will one day flower into its fullness. What is now partial will become complete. What is now hidden will be revealed.

This raises an even larger question. If the destiny of rational creatures is union with God, what must that union be like? Can anything weighed down by corruption, division, or limitation fully share in the life of the One who is beyond all corruption? Here the argument begins to move toward one of its boldest conclusions. The end of the journey cannot merely be a repaired version of our present condition. It must be a transformation into a mode of life capable of sharing in God's own life. The image points forward to the likeness, and the likeness points forward to a union so complete that God becomes, in the apostle's words, "all in all."

The story of creation, then, is not simply the story of where humanity came from. It is the story of what humanity is becoming. We begin with the image of God already planted within us. The entire journey of existence is the unfolding of that gift until, at last, the likeness hidden within the image is brought to completion.
