## 7 — The Light and Its Radiance

> *“He is the brightness of the eternal light and the image of his goodness.”*

If Christ is the eternal Wisdom of God, why does Scripture give him so many different names? He is called Wisdom, Word, Truth, Life, Way, Resurrection, Light, Son, and Firstborn. At first this may seem confusing, as though Scripture were describing many different realities. Yet these names are not meant to divide Christ into parts. They are different windows through which we glimpse the same reality.

Think of a mountain viewed from different directions. One traveler sees forests along its slopes. Another notices cliffs and waterfalls. A third admires the snow-covered summit. Each description is true, yet none captures the whole mountain. In a similar way, Christ is called Life because all living things receive their life through him. He is called Truth because everything that truly exists participates in him. He is called the Way because he leads wandering souls back toward their source. Each title reveals something real, yet none exhausts the mystery.

This leads to a deeper question. If the Son comes from the Father, when did that happen? Was there a time when God existed without the Son? The argument presented here is remarkably simple. If there were ever a time when the Son did not exist, there would have been a time when God lacked Wisdom, Truth, and Word. Yet how could the eternal God ever be without wisdom? How could the source of all reason ever be without reason? The very idea seems self-contradictory.

For this reason, the relationship between Father and Son cannot be understood as one event among others. It is not something that happened at a particular moment. Rather, it belongs to the eternal life of God. The Son is eternally from the Father, just as radiance is eternally from light. When a lamp shines, its brightness is not produced after a delay. The light and its radiance belong together. You can distinguish them in thought, but you cannot separate them in reality.

Yet this comparison must be handled carefully. When we hear words like "birth" or "begetting," we naturally imagine physical processes. We think of parents and children, bodies and beginnings. But divine realities cannot be reduced to earthly ones. God does not divide himself into pieces. The Son is not a fragment broken off from the Father, nor a lesser being standing at a distance from him. The relationship is far more profound than any human comparison can fully express.

To help us grasp this mystery, let's turn to the idea of an image. A faithful image reveals what it reflects. When you look into a perfectly clear mirror, your attention is not drawn to the glass itself but to the reality made visible through it. In a similar way, the Son reveals the Father. Not because the Father is absent and needs representation, but because the Father is beyond direct comprehension. Through the Son, the hidden becomes knowable. Through the visible life of Christ, the invisible nature of God is disclosed.

This is why Christ is called both the Truth and the Image. He is the Truth because he shares fully in the reality of God. He is the Image because he makes that reality accessible to us. When we seek to understand Christ, we are not being distracted from God. We are being led toward him. The radiance reveals the light. The image reveals the original. The Word reveals the mind from which it proceeds. And through that revelation, the unknowable God becomes, in some measure, known.
