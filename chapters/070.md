## 70 — One Lump, Many Journeys

> *"From the same lump to make one vessel unto honor, and another unto dishonor."*

Having argued that human choices matter, let's return to the image of the potter and the clay. If all people come from the same lump, why do their lives unfold so differently? Why does one person seem drawn toward wisdom while another struggles continually with destructive habits? Why does one appear to begin life with advantages that another lacks?

The first point is that all rational beings share the same fundamental nature. The difference between people is not that some were created from superior material while others were made from something defective. The clay is the same. Whatever distinctions exist must therefore arise from something other than a difference in their essential nature.

This allows the argument to reject the idea that there are two kinds of souls—one destined for salvation and another destined for ruin. If all come from the same lump and all are fashioned by the same Creator, then no one can claim that goodness belongs naturally to one class of beings while wickedness belongs naturally to another. The possibility of growth, decline, repentance, and transformation remains open to all.

That conviction also shapes how difficult passages such as Jacob being loved and Esau being hated are understood. Rather than seeing these as arbitrary choices made before either person had done anything, the suggestion is that there are causes deeper than what is visible in a single lifetime. One answer here rests upon a broader belief that the soul's story extends beyond the brief span of earthly existence. Whether one accepts that conclusion or not, the purpose of the argument is clear: to defend the justice of God by insisting that divine judgments are never arbitrary.

An equally important point follows from this. If people become vessels of honor or dishonor because of choices, character, and conduct, then those conditions are not necessarily permanent. A person who presently lives honorably may later abandon what is good. Likewise, someone who has become deeply entangled in destructive patterns may yet change. The labels are not fixed identities. They describe a present condition rather than an unalterable destiny.

This perspective transforms how we view ourselves and others. It discourages pride because no one can assume their current state guarantees their future. It also discourages despair because no one is trapped forever by their present failures. Human beings remain capable of movement. They can drift away from the good, but they can also return to it. The story is still being written.

Here is a subtle observation about questioning God. Honest questions are not condemned. Moses spoke with God openly. The issue is not inquiry itself but the spirit in which it is made. There is a difference between asking in order to understand and asking merely to accuse. One posture seeks wisdom; the other seeks ammunition. The search for truth requires humility, a willingness to learn rather than merely to win an argument.

The larger vision that emerges is one of profound moral freedom. We are not divided into fixed categories of saved and lost by nature. We share a common origin, a common dignity, and a common capacity for transformation. The clay is one. What differs is the shape it gradually takes as life unfolds and as each soul responds to the opportunities placed before it.
