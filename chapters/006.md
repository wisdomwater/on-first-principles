## 6 — The Eternal Wisdom

> *“Christ, the power of God and the wisdom of God.”*

When we hear the name Jesus, we naturally think of a particular moment in history: a child born in Bethlehem, a teacher walking the roads of Galilee, a man crucified under Roman authority. Yet if Christ is truly the Son of God, we must ask a deeper question. Who was he before any of those events took place?

One answer might be that God existed alone for a time and later brought forth the Son. But follow that thought carefully. If there was ever a moment when God lacked wisdom, then God would have changed from being without wisdom to possessing it. And if God already possessed the power to bring forth wisdom but simply chose not to, then wisdom would have been absent by choice rather than necessity. Neither possibility seems fitting for the source of all perfection.

Instead, consider what it means to call Christ the Wisdom of God. Wisdom is not something God acquires. It belongs to God's very being. Just as light radiates from a flame and cannot be separated from it, divine wisdom cannot be separated from God. Therefore, if God has always been God, then divine wisdom has always been with God. The Son is not an afterthought in the story of creation but an eternal reality within the life of God himself.

This helps explain why Scripture gives Christ many names. He is called Wisdom because all understanding originates in him. He is called the Firstborn because all creation comes through him. He is called the Word because he expresses and reveals the hidden depths of the divine mind. Think of a thought becoming speech. Before the words are spoken, the meaning already exists within the mind. The spoken word makes that meaning known. In a similar way, Christ is the eternal self-expression of God, revealing what would otherwise remain hidden.

Before the universe existed in visible form, all its possibilities already existed within divine wisdom. An architect holds an entire building in mind before the first stone is laid. A composer hears the symphony inwardly before a single note is played. In the same way, the patterns, purposes, and possibilities of creation existed first within the wisdom of God. Creation unfolds in time, but its meaning is rooted in an eternal source.

This understanding changes how we view the world around us. The universe is not the result of accident or blind chance. It emerges from wisdom. Beneath the complexity of life, beneath the movement of history, beneath the countless forms of existence, there is an underlying intelligibility. Reality is not chaos at its deepest level. It is ordered by meaning.

For this reason Christ is not only the source of creation but also its truth and life. Everything that truly exists participates, however faintly, in the reality that flows from him. Every glimpse of truth, every experience of beauty, every movement toward goodness reflects something of the eternal Wisdom through whom all things were made. To seek wisdom, then, is already to move toward Christ, who is not merely a figure within creation but the living source from which creation itself arises.
