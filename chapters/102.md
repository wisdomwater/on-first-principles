## 102 — When the Story Points Beyond Itself

> *“These statements are made in a figurative manner, in order that through them certain mystical truths may be indicated.”*

How do you know when a story is meant to be read literally and when it is pointing beyond itself? Most of us instinctively understand this in everyday life. If someone says, “My heart is broken,” we do not imagine a damaged organ. We recognize that the language is describing a deeper reality through a visible image. The words are not less true because they are symbolic. In some ways, they are more powerful because they communicate something that ordinary description struggles to express.

The same principle can apply to scripture. Sometimes readers encounter passages that seem difficult to reconcile with a straightforward, literal reading. Consider the opening chapters of Genesis. How can there be days and nights before the sun and moon exist? What kind of tree could grant life simply by being eaten? How does someone hide from God behind a tree? Questions like these are not necessarily obstacles to faith. They may be invitations to read more deeply.

When we encounter such passages, we can ask whether the story is trying to communicate something greater than the events themselves. The garden may be teaching us about humanity's relationship with God. The tree of life may be revealing something about the source of true life. Adam hiding among the trees may be exposing a tendency that still exists within us—the impulse to withdraw, conceal ourselves, and avoid the light when we feel ashamed. The narrative remains meaningful, but its deepest value lies in what it reveals about the human condition and our spiritual journey.

This way of reading extends beyond Genesis. Scripture often contains moments that seem deliberately designed to awaken deeper reflection. Even in the Gospels, there are scenes that raise questions when examined closely. For example, how could a single mountain provide a view of every kingdom on earth? Perhaps the point of the story is not geographical but spiritual. The focus is not the height of the mountain but the nature of the temptation. The reader is being shown the allure of power, glory, and domination that confronts every human soul.

The danger is that we can become so occupied with defending the surface details that we miss the treasure hidden beneath them. It is like spending all our time examining the frame of a painting while never looking at the image itself. The purpose of scripture is not merely to provide information about past events. Its deeper purpose is to illuminate realities that are always present—truths about God, humanity, freedom, temptation, growth, and transformation.

This does not mean the stories are meaningless. Quite the opposite. Their power comes from their ability to carry truths that are larger than themselves. A story can be a vessel. An image can become a doorway. A narrative can lead the reader into a wisdom that cannot easily be expressed in direct statements alone. When a passage seems strange or puzzling, the question may not be, "How do I force this to fit my expectations?" but rather, "What deeper truth is this story inviting me to see?"
