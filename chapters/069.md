## 69 — The Potter and the Clay

> *"If a man purge himself, he shall be a vessel unto honor."*

The image of the potter and the clay has troubled readers for centuries. If a potter makes one vessel for honor and another for dishonor, does that mean some people are created for goodness while others are created for ruin? If so, what becomes of justice, responsibility, or freedom?

This is exactly the question being confronted here. On the surface, the image seems to suggest that everything is decided in advance. If the potter alone determines the destiny of each vessel, then praise and blame become meaningless. Why commend a person for virtue if they were made that way from the beginning? Why condemn another for wrongdoing if they never had the possibility of becoming anything else?

The difficulty becomes even greater when we consider the rest of Scripture. The same apostle who uses the image of the potter also rebukes wrongdoers, encourages the faithful, praises acts of courage, and warns against sin. He speaks as though human choices genuinely matter. He tells people to repent, persevere, and pursue what is good. None of this would make sense if every action were simply the unavoidable result of how a person had been created.

A clue appears in another image from the same apostle. In a great house, he says, there are vessels of many kinds. Some are used for honorable purposes and some for ordinary ones. Yet he immediately adds that if a person cleanses themselves, they can become a vessel for honorable use. Notice what this implies. The vessel is not trapped in a fixed destiny. Something about its condition can change. The difference between honor and dishonor is connected to purification, not merely to how the vessel was first shaped.

The argument, then, is that God is not arbitrarily assigning people to opposite destinies. Rather, people become the kind of vessel they are through the choices they make and the condition of their character. The potter remains the creator of every vessel, but the vessel's fitness for one purpose or another is related to what it has become. A neglected tool rusts. A well-maintained tool remains useful. The maker is not responsible for every difference that emerges afterward.

This preserves both divine justice and human responsibility. If people are judged, rewarded, corrected, or praised, there must be something meaningful about the way they have lived. Otherwise, judgment would be little more than assigning consequences for a role someone was forced to play. The entire moral life assumes that our choices participate in shaping who we become.

The deeper point is that human beings are not static objects. We are always in the process of becoming. Every choice leaves a mark. Every act of honesty, generosity, resentment, selfishness, courage, or love contributes to the kind of vessel we are forming ourselves into. The Creator provides the clay, the life, the opportunities, and the guidance. Yet what emerges from that material is not independent of our participation. We are being shaped, but we are also responding to the shaping. And it is in that mysterious partnership that character—and destiny—are formed.
