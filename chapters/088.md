## 88 — The Signature of a Living Word

> *"The whole world has been filled with his teaching."*

If someone claimed to possess a message from God, how could that claim be tested? One possibility would be to ask whether the message carries within it a power that exceeds what human effort alone could reasonably accomplish. This is the line of thought being explored here.

The argument begins by observing a remarkable pattern. Again and again, the ancient scriptures describe events that seem unlikely from a merely human perspective. They speak of old religious structures passing away, of people once considered outsiders being welcomed into God's purposes, and of a coming figure whose influence would extend far beyond the boundaries of a single nation. These claims would have sounded improbable when they were first spoken. Yet, viewed through the lens of subsequent history, they appear strikingly relevant.

One example concerns the surprising inclusion of people who had previously stood outside the covenant life of Israel. The scriptures speak of a "foolish nation" being called by God—a people not known for religious privilege or spiritual prestige. The point is not that these people were actually foolish. Rather, they were regarded that way by those who believed themselves to possess special status. Yet history unfolded in such a way that countless people from distant nations embraced the God of Israel through Christ. The boundaries that once seemed fixed began to dissolve.

This pattern reveals something important about how God works. Human beings often expect greatness to emerge from the powerful, the educated, the influential, and the respected. Yet divine wisdom frequently appears through unexpected channels. The movement that transformed the ancient world did not begin among emperors, philosophers, or military leaders. It spread among ordinary people. Fishermen, laborers, merchants, widows, slaves, and strangers became carriers of a message that crossed cultural and political barriers. What seemed weak became strong. What seemed insignificant became influential.

We then turn to the many prophecies that Christians saw fulfilled in the life of Jesus. Particular attention is given to a recurring theme: the extraordinary reach of a ministry that, by ordinary standards, should have remained small. Jesus taught publicly for only a brief period. He left behind no political movement, commanded no army, and wrote no books. Yet his teachings spread throughout the world. This fact itself becomes part of the evidence. The disproportion between the apparent cause and the resulting effect suggested the presence of something greater than human strategy.

The same reasoning applies to the prophetic descriptions of a kingdom characterized by righteousness, peace, and worldwide influence. These images were not interpreted merely as poetic aspirations. They were seen as signs pointing toward a reality that was already beginning to unfold. The message of Christ continued advancing long after his earthly ministry ended, reaching peoples and places that would have been unimaginable to his earliest followers.

The deeper question beneath all of this is not merely whether specific predictions were fulfilled. It is whether history itself bears the signature of a living word. A merely human idea often fades when its creator is gone. A living word continues working long after its first speaker has fallen silent. It crosses generations, languages, and cultures, carrying a vitality that seems out of proportion to its origins. The argument presented here is that Scripture possesses precisely this quality. Its enduring power is not simply one more fact about history. It is one of the reasons it deserves to be heard.
