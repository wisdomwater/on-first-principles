## 56 — Why Commands Matter

> *“I have set before thee the way of life and the way of death. Choose the good and walk in it.”*

Imagine giving instructions to someone who had absolutely no ability to respond. It would be like commanding the wind to stop blowing or ordering a stone to change its course. Commands only make sense when addressed to a being capable of hearing, weighing, and choosing.

This observation forms one of the strongest foundations for the reality of human freedom. Throughout Scripture, people are continually called to choose. They are urged to listen, obey, turn, repent, persevere, love, forgive, and walk in the right path. These appeals are not directed toward passive objects but toward persons. The very existence of such commands assumes that something within us is capable of responding.

This does not mean that choosing what is good is always easy. Often it is difficult. We are influenced by desires, habits, fears, and circumstances. Yet the call remains meaningful precisely because we are not completely governed by those things. A teacher does not ask a student to solve a problem unless learning is possible. A parent does not urge a child toward maturity unless growth can occur. In the same way, the repeated invitations of Scripture assume that human beings possess the capacity to cooperate with what is good.

The same principle appears in the language of judgment. Praise and blame only make sense where freedom exists. When someone acts with courage, generosity, or integrity, we recognize that something worthy has taken place. When someone chooses cruelty, dishonesty, or selfishness, we hold them accountable. Our ordinary moral language depends upon the assumption that human beings are more than machines responding automatically to stimuli. We believe people could have acted differently, and therefore their choices matter.

The Apostle Paul reinforces this point when he speaks of people receiving the consequences of their actions. His argument assumes that human beings participate in the formation of their own character. Through repeated choices they become the kind of person who moves either toward wisdom or away from it. The future is not presented as something imposed upon them from outside but as something connected to how they respond to the opportunities placed before them.

Yet there is an obvious difficulty. Certain passages of Scripture seem to say the opposite. Some texts speak as though God hardens a person's heart or determines events in ways that appear to leave little room for human freedom. If such passages are taken in a particular way, they seem to challenge everything that has been argued so far. Rather than ignoring these difficulties, the next step is to face them directly. Genuine understanding is not afraid of hard questions. If free will is real, it must be able to withstand the strongest objections raised against it. And so the discussion now turns from building the case for freedom to examining the passages that seem to deny it.
